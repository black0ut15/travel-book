# Voice Fingerprint — Investing from the Inside

## Sentence Length & Rhythm

Sentences are short to medium, punchy, and declarative.
They land one idea at a time.
Longer sentences appear when walking through a process or example, but even then they don't meander.
The rhythm is confident — statement, then explanation, then move on.

## Vocabulary

Plain, working-class American English.
No academic jargon, no inflated diction.
When a technical term must be introduced (P/E ratio, ex-dividend date, fiduciary), it gets defined immediately in plain language.
Slang and prison vernacular appear naturally — "down for 22 years," "a dime," "holiday with the DOC," "baby momma," "toilet paper" — and are never self-conscious.
Profanity appears occasionally for emphasis and feels earned, not edgy.

## Directness

The author writes as someone talking at you, not to you in a polished way.
Commands are common: "Don't put all your eggs in one basket." "Make sure you have set a stop loss."
There's no hedging, no throat-clearing.
Opinions are stated as facts when the author believes them to be true.

## Explaining Technical Concepts

New concepts follow a consistent pattern: name it → define it in plain language → show a concrete example (often numerical) → state the practical implication.
Abstract ideas always get grounded in something the reader can picture from prison life (commissary spending, phone call budgets, cellmates, yard time).
The author doesn't dumb things down — he translates them.

## Tone

Candid, self-aware, and occasionally blunt to the point of irreverence.
The author admits his own mistakes openly (the BRCC story, impulse control, his ADD) and uses them as teaching tools.
There's a persistent undercurrent of "I've been where you are, and I'm not going to BS you."
Motivational without being preachy — more drill sergeant than life coach.
Humor surfaces drily: "If only I hadn't robbed that liquor store."

## Perspective

First-person throughout.
Personal anecdotes drive credibility.
The author positions himself as a peer who got something right, not an expert looking down.

## Prompt Usage Notes

When drafting with Claude, paste this file into the Claude Project as a reference document.
Use this prompt prefix when generating new sections:

> "Write in the voice described in voice_fingerprint.md. Short punchy sentences. Plain language. First person. Translate any financial concept using a prison/commissary analogy before giving the real-world example. No hedging. No filler words like 'delve' or 'tapestry'."

After each AI generation, check for:
- [ ] Sentences that meander or over-explain
- [ ] Academic or corporate phrasing ("leverage synergies," "it is important to note")
- [ ] Missing concrete numerical examples
- [ ] Missing first-person grounding
- [ ] Filler words: delve, tapestry, crucial, it's worth noting, in conclusion
