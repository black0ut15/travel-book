# NO CLEAN SLATE
## Chapter 7: U.S. Territories — A Special Category

---

The five U.S. territories — Puerto Rico, Guam, the U.S. Virgin Islands, the Northern Mariana Islands, and American Samoa — sit in a legal category that does not fit neatly into either the domestic or international chapters of this book. They are not states. They are not foreign countries. No passport is required to travel there. But each has its own legislature, its own laws, and its own registration rules — and a person who assumes they operate like a domestic state, or like an international destination, will likely be wrong on both counts.

This chapter belongs in the domestic section because no passport is required. It gets its own chapter because the rules are genuinely different from state-to-state travel, different from international travel, and different from each other.

---

## The Legal Framework

U.S. citizens can travel to any of the five territories without a passport. There is no visa process, no immigration screening in the traditional sense, no customs declaration as if crossing an international border. The territories are part of the United States for purposes of travel.

What they are not is states. Territories have their own legislative bodies and enact their own laws. They are subject to federal law — including SORNA and the Adam Walsh Act — but are not governed by the laws of any state. Each territory has developed its own sex offender registration scheme, with its own tier system, its own residency rules, and its own AWA compliance status.

For felons who are off paper: the five territories all allow U.S. citizens to visit and relocate without documented criminal record entry barriers. There is no territorial equivalent of Canada's bar on felons at the border. An off-paper felon can travel to Puerto Rico or the U.S. Virgin Islands the same way they travel to Florida.

For registered persons: registration obligations apply in each territory, triggered by the length or purpose of the stay. The specific rules vary by territory and are covered below.

---

## Territory-by-Territory: RSO Registration Rules

### Puerto Rico (PR)

Puerto Rico is the only U.S. territory not substantially AWA compliant.[1] It uses a standard three-tier system — Tier I registrants check in annually for 15 years; Tier II biannually for 25 years; Tier III and those designated as "Dangerous Sexual Offenders" quarterly for life — but the underlying classification and registration mechanics are governed by Puerto Rico's own statutes rather than the federal AWA framework.

Visitors are subject to registration obligations if they come to Puerto Rico for work or study. Those convicted in other jurisdictions who are in Puerto Rico "by reasons of work or study" must register within 3 calendar days of arrival, even if they do not intend to establish a permanent residence.[2] The statute does not limit this requirement to extended stays — the work or study trigger applies regardless of intended duration.

Puerto Rico requires 21 days advance notice for international travel. Residence restrictions apply to those with offenses against minors: 500 feet from elementary, intermediate, and high schools, and from certified child daycare establishments.

One notable civil rights feature: Puerto Rico imposes no voting restrictions for criminal convictions. Residents who are U.S. citizens can vote in territorial elections without restriction based on conviction history, though — like all territory residents — they cannot vote in federal elections.

---

### Guam (GU)

Guam is AWA compliant and uses a tier system, but the numbering runs opposite to every mainland state and most other territories.[3] This is not a minor administrative quirk. On the mainland, Level I or Tier I is the lowest classification — the least restrictive, the shortest registration period. In Guam, Level I is the highest classification — quarterly registration for life. Level III is the lowest, with annual registration for 15 years.

A Tier I registrant from a mainland state who assumes "Level I equals lowest risk" in Guam is wrong by the maximum possible margin. A mainland Tier I — who registers annually and may have a defined path to petition for removal — becomes a Guam Level I subject to quarterly for-life registration upon establishing residency. Know this before considering Guam.

"Resides" in Guam is defined as being present in the jurisdiction for 30 or more days. Visitors who do not reach that threshold are not required to register, but those who do establish residence are subject to the full registration framework. Guam has no statewide residency restrictions — registrants are not prohibited from living within a certain distance of schools or parks. The 21-day international travel notice is required under Guam law. Employment restrictions apply: registrants cannot work at medical, dental, or physical therapy facilities while patients are present; child care facilities while children are present; schools while children are present; hospital nursing homes or hospice facilities while patients are present; or in-home service roles where the employee enters private residences.

---

### U.S. Virgin Islands (VI)

The U.S. Virgin Islands is AWA compliant and uses the standard tier system: Tier I annually for 15 years, Tier II biannually for 25 years, Tier III quarterly for life.[4]

The USVI has a specific visitor notification rule worth knowing. Registered persons who will be present in the territory for less than 30 days in any given year are not required to formally register but must contact the VI Department of Justice to notify it of their presence, including all arrival and departure information.[5] This is a contact requirement, not a full registration — but it is a legal obligation for any visiting registered person regardless of the length of the stay.

The USVI has no statewide residency restrictions and no presence restrictions. Employment restrictions apply: registrants cannot operate or work at schools, daycare centers, after-school programs, summer programs or camps, or any place deemed a child care facility. The 21-day international travel notice is codified in USVI law.

Relief is available: Tier I registrants can petition for a reduction in their registration period after 10 consecutive clean years, subject to a favorable determination by the SO Registry Board on risk of re-offense. Tier III registrants who were adjudicated as juveniles can petition for reduction after 25 clean years under the same process.

---

### Northern Mariana Islands (MP)

The Commonwealth of the Northern Mariana Islands is a chain of 14 islands in the Western Pacific — only three are inhabited — located approximately 2,000 miles from the Asian continent and just north of Guam.[6] It is AWA compliant and uses the standard tier system (Tier I annually for 15 years; Tier II biannually for 25 years; Tier III quarterly for life).

Two features of CNMI registration stand out. First, the international travel advance notice requirement is only 3 days — significantly less restrictive than the 21-day requirement on the mainland and in most other territories.[7] For a registered person who needs flexibility in international travel planning, this is a meaningful practical difference from most U.S. jurisdictions.

Second, the CNMI has a "recapture" provision: if a registered person is released following any offense — not just a sex offense — the territory can add them to the registry if they have a prior sex offense conviction in their history.[8] This provision can trap registrants who believed they had completed their registration obligations.

The CNMI has residency restrictions: registrants cannot reside within 1,000 feet of playgrounds, schools, school bus stops, community centers, or other locations designated primarily for minors. Registrants must also give notice if they are away from their registered address for 72 or more hours.

---

### American Samoa (AS)

American Samoa was the last of the five territories to establish a registry, doing so in 2015, and is AWA compliant.[9] Its registration scheme is more limited in scope than most U.S. jurisdictions. Registration appears to apply primarily to offenses involving minor victims — the statute focuses on "covered offenses against victims who are minors or of sexually violent offenses" — with sexually violent predators and those with two or more convictions subject to lifetime registration.

The territorial statutes do not include a specific provision for visiting registrants. American Samoa has no statewide residency restrictions, no employment restrictions, and no presence restrictions. The 21-day international travel notice is enforced under federal law even though it is not codified in territorial law.

One civic distinction applies here that does not apply to the other territories: American Samoa is the only U.S. territory whose residents are classified as U.S. nationals rather than U.S. citizens by birth. This has implications for certain federal benefits and some travel documents, though it does not affect travel within the United States or to other territories. The territorial voting laws note that those who lose voting rights due to a felony conviction can appeal the ban in a territorial court hearing — an unusual appellate pathway.

---

## The 21-Day IML Notice and Territorial Travel

International Megan's Law requires registered persons to give 21 days advance notice before traveling "outside of the United States." The five territories are legally part of the United States for this purpose. Traveling from a mainland state to Puerto Rico or Guam does not trigger the IML 21-day notice requirement — the territories are not international destinations under the statute.[10]

What does trigger the requirement is any international leg of travel. A registered person who flies from Los Angeles to Honolulu to Guam is traveling domestically the entire way — all three locations are U.S. jurisdictions. But a registered person who flies from Guam to Japan, or from Puerto Rico to the Dominican Republic, is traveling internationally from a U.S. jurisdiction, and the 21-day notice requirement applies to the international destination.

The practical implication: territories can serve as staging points for regional activity without triggering the IML notice mechanism on their own. But any travel that leaves U.S. jurisdiction — from a territory or from a state — still requires the full 21-day notice to the home registry office for the international destination.

## For Felons: What the Territories Offer

For off-paper felons, the territories are among the most accessible destinations covered in this book. No passport is required. No entry bar exists for U.S. citizen felons. The territories do not screen arriving U.S. citizens for felony records at ports of entry. The record will surface at any point of law enforcement contact — that is true everywhere — but the baseline access is unrestricted.

This has practical value. A felon who wants to experience the Caribbean without the entry complications that foreign islands present — Jamaica bars some felons, the Bahamas may screen, many Caribbean nations are unpredictable — can travel to Puerto Rico or the U.S. Virgin Islands with none of those barriers. A felon interested in a Pacific destination without the entry complications of Japan or Australia can consider Guam or the CNMI.

For long-term relocation, the territories follow territory law on employment and housing. Professional licensing in the territories operates under territorial rules, not state rules. A felon researching employment opportunities should treat each territory as its own jurisdiction and research accordingly. The economic realities vary significantly: Puerto Rico has faced prolonged fiscal and infrastructure challenges; Guam has a strong economy anchored in the U.S. military presence and tourism; the USVI depends heavily on tourism; American Samoa has limited economic infrastructure and is geographically isolated; the CNMI has its own economic dynamics shaped by tourism and geographic location.

---

## Civic Limitations: What Territory Residency Costs

Moving to a U.S. territory preserves U.S. citizenship and most constitutional rights. What it removes is federal voting representation. Residents of U.S. territories cannot vote in presidential elections and are represented in Congress only by non-voting delegates.[11] This is not a collateral consequence of a criminal record — it is a structural feature of territorial status that applies to all residents regardless of their history. For a felon weighing a territory relocation against a state relocation, the loss of federal voting rights is a real civic cost, separate from any record-based voting restriction.

For registered persons considering a territorial move specifically as a strategy for managing registration obligations: the territories are U.S. jurisdictions subject to SORNA, they all have registration requirements, and the AWA compliance framework applies. There is no territory where a person required to register in a mainland state can relocate and simply stop registering. The dual notification requirement from Chapter 6 applies here as well — notify the sending state and register in the receiving territory.

---

## Takeaway

The five U.S. territories are neither states nor foreign countries, and the rules that apply to people with criminal records reflect that hybrid status. For felons off paper, they are accessible domestic destinations and viable relocation options without the barriers of international travel. For registered persons, each has its own registration system that requires research before visiting or moving.

Three specific points to carry forward. First, passports are not required — but registration obligations may apply. Second, Guam's tier numbering is inverted relative to the mainland — Level I in Guam is the highest classification, not the lowest. Third, the IML 21-day notice does not apply to territorial travel itself, but it applies to any international travel that departs from a territory.
