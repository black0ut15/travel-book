# NO CLEAN SLATE
## Chapter 10: Relocating Abroad — Long-Term and Permanent

---

Most people who research leaving the United States as a felon or registered person are looking for relief from something — the registry, the collateral consequences, the daily weight of a record that follows them into every job application, lease, and background check. The impulse is understandable. The domestic landscape covered in the previous twelve chapters is genuinely difficult.

But emigration is not an escape hatch. It is a serious immigration undertaking — one that subjects the applicant to the same criminal record scrutiny as any other long-term visa or permanent residency application, except applied by countries whose standards at the immigration stage are often more restrictive than what they apply to tourists. The country that waves an American tourist through with no questions may require a clean criminal record certification for a work visa. The country that would let an RSO visit may automatically exclude that person from long-term residency.

This chapter covers what legal long-term relocation actually looks like, what obligations follow an American abroad, what the asylum cases actually established, and what an honest assessment of the emigration option looks like for most people reading this book.

---

## Immigration Is Not Tourism

Tourist entry — the subject of Chapters 8 and 9 — operates at the border. A passport is scanned, a form is reviewed, a decision is made in minutes. Long-term residency and permanent immigration operate through a formal application process: visa applications, background check requirements, character certifications from law enforcement, financial documentation, sponsor letters, language requirements, and in many cases proof of skills or income that justify the destination country's investment in accepting a new resident.

The screening gap between tourist entry and immigration is wide. A person with a felony conviction who can enter a country for a two-week vacation may be entirely ineligible for a long-term visa from the same country. The standards apply differently at different points of entry into a country's immigration system.

Most countries' immigration frameworks mirror the U.S. approach in one respect: they prefer immigrants who bring resources, skills, or demonstrated capacity to contribute. Legal immigration globally tends to apply four broad categories of assessment: health, criminal history, security profile, and immigration law compliance history. A person with a serious criminal record faces scrutiny on the second category from the first form they submit.[1]

The most viable legal pathway for most people is employment-based immigration: a work visa, employer sponsorship or self-employed status, and eventually permanent residency or naturalization. This pathway requires a skill set that a foreign employer needs, an employer willing to navigate the sponsorship process, and a criminal record that does not trigger the destination country's disqualification rules. For RSOs with offenses against minors, many countries apply automatic bars at the immigration stage that they may not enforce uniformly at the tourist border.

Retirement and passive income visas offer an alternative pathway in some countries. These programs require demonstrated income or assets and are specifically designed to attract foreign retirees. However, most run criminal record checks — the key variable is what they do with the results.

Three programs commonly cited by expat communities illustrate the range:

**Portugal's D7 Passive Income Visa** requires an FBI background check (apostilled) from U.S. applicants. Minor offenses punishable by less than one year under Portuguese law are generally permitted. More serious offenses — particularly those punishable by more than one year in prison — are likely to result in denial. The income threshold is approximately €920 per month (indexed to Portugal's minimum wage). This is one of the more accessible programs for people with minor records, but it is not a clean-record-free pathway.

**Mexico's temporary resident visa** is more variable. Some immigration consultants report that background checks are not a formal requirement for the residency application itself, and Mexican law gives immigration authorities discretionary rather than categorical authority to deny based on criminal record. In practice, work visas explicitly require clean records, and immigration officers can deny entry or residency on criminal grounds. For a felon without a work visa application, the outcome is not predictable. The income threshold for financial-solvency-based temporary residency is roughly equivalent to demonstrating consistent monthly bank balances.

**Panama's Pensionado visa** requires an apostilled FBI background check and categorically bars anyone with a felony on their record — regardless of how long ago the conviction occurred. The income threshold is $1,000 per month from a lifetime pension source. Despite its reputation as a retiree-friendly destination, Panama is effectively closed to felons seeking long-term residency.

The broader lesson: retirement visas are worth researching, but the criminal record requirement should be the first thing verified for any specific program, not an assumption in either direction. Programs change, consulates interpret rules differently, and immigration attorneys in the destination country are the authoritative source.

## What Stays Behind — and What Follows

Moving abroad does not sever American legal obligations. Several of them follow the person across the border.

### For Registered Sex Offenders

The registry obligation before departure is non-negotiable. Most states have explicitly codified the requirement to register before leaving the country. A person who departs without registering generates FTR exposure. The 21-day IML notice requirement covers international departures — it is not waived because the trip is permanent. File the notice. Register before leaving.

Once abroad, the IML notification system continues to operate. Any subsequent international travel — moving from one country to another, returning to the U.S. to visit, passing through a third country — routes through the Angel Watch Center pipeline and notifies foreign authorities of the RSO's status. There is no mechanism for opting out of IML by relocating abroad. The system follows the person.

Some states continue to list RSOs on their public registries after the person has left — Florida and New York are among those documented as maintaining public listings for departed registrants. The public listing does not create a continuing obligation to register if the person has legally departed and met all pre-departure requirements. But it does mean that the person's information remains publicly visible on a U.S. government-maintained website accessible from anywhere in the world, including the destination country.

One claim that circulates in RSO communities is that the U.S. Embassy in the destination country requires ongoing registration while the person lives abroad. As of the most current information available, there is no verified evidence that this is correct. The claim is not confirmed as U.S. law or policy.[2]

### For All U.S. Citizens Abroad

**Social Security:** U.S. citizens living abroad generally may continue to collect Social Security retirement benefits. The SSA will send payments to most foreign countries. There are exceptions — payments cannot be sent to Cuba, North Korea, and a small number of sanctioned or restricted destinations. Felony convictions and RSO status do not currently bar Social Security retirement benefits.[3]

**Federal tax filing:** U.S. citizens are required to file federal tax returns regardless of where they live. Unlike most other countries, the U.S. taxes its citizens on worldwide income. The Foreign Earned Income Exclusion reduces the tax burden for most long-term expats, but the filing obligation persists. This is a permanent feature of U.S. citizenship that does not go away upon departure and is a practical consideration for anyone planning long-term foreign residence.

**Renouncing citizenship:** Some people researching emigration encounter references to renouncing U.S. citizenship as a way to sever all remaining ties to U.S. law. Renunciation is real — it is an irreversible legal process conducted at a U.S. consulate abroad — but it is not a practical solution for most people. It eliminates the U.S. passport, eliminates the right to reenter the U.S. as a citizen, triggers an exit tax on certain assets, and does not eliminate U.S. tax filing obligations for up to 10 years after renunciation for qualifying taxpayers. It also requires that the person have already established legal status in another country — you cannot renounce citizenship without somewhere else to be a citizen of. Most people researching emigration as a practical option will not reach the point where renunciation is relevant.[4]

## The Extradition Question

The question that comes up in every forum discussion of emigration: what if I just move to a country that doesn't have an extradition treaty with the United States?

The U.S. has extradition treaties with 107 of 193 UN-recognized nations as of July 2024.[5] The absence of a treaty does not mean the absence of extradition. Countries can and do extradite without formal treaties on a case-by-case basis. Several countries that have treaties with the U.S. are known to often refuse specific extradition requests anyway — Ecuador, Cuba, Bolivia, Nicaragua, Iceland, Switzerland, Venezuela, and Zimbabwe among them. Countries like Spain and Yemen have returned fugitives without any formal treaty obligation.

Most non-treaty countries are in regions that are problematic for American emigrants for reasons unrelated to extradition: parts of Africa with significant instability, former Soviet nations, the Middle East, and Southeast Asia. The countries without diplomatic ties to the U.S. at all — Bhutan, Iran, North Korea, Palestine, Syria, Taiwan, and Western Sahara — are not viable emigration destinations.

Fleeing to a non-extradition country to avoid an FTR charge does not eliminate the charge. The federal FTR prosecution continues in absentia. Upon any return to U.S. soil — including through a third country — the warrant remains active. The person becomes a fugitive under U.S. law, subject to immediate arrest. The choice is not "FTR charge versus freedom abroad." It is "FTR charge resolved through the legal system" versus "FTR charge plus permanent fugitive status plus restricted movement for the rest of one's life." YLOTL's position on this is direct: do it legally or don't do it. Fleeing to evade the registry may put the person in greater danger than the registry itself.[6]

## Asylum: What the Cases Actually Established

Political asylum is a formal legal status granted by a foreign government when the applicant demonstrates that returning to their home country would result in persecution — specifically, torture, cruel and unusual punishment, or death — based on race, religion, nationality, political opinion, or membership in a particular social group. It is not a general hardship exemption and is not a loophole through which difficult domestic circumstances can be escaped.

The documented U.S. RSO asylum cases are few. They are also frequently misunderstood.

**Denise Harvey (Canada, 2014).** A Florida teacher convicted of sex with a 16-year-old received a 30-year sentence. She fled to Canada, where she was granted "protected person" status — not asylum in the traditional sense, but a status recognizing that extradition would expose her to punishment Canada considered cruel and unusual. Two specific factors drove the outcome: Canada viewed the 30-year sentence as excessive, and the underlying conduct was not a crime under Canadian law. Harvey remains wanted under U.S. law. She cannot set foot in the United States.[7]

**Steven Whitsett (Germany, 2022).** Whitsett fled Florida to Germany and was granted asylum — but not because Germany condemned the U.S. sex offense registry. The German court explicitly stated the registry served a legitimate public purpose. Asylum was granted because Florida had communicated to German authorities that Whitsett faced 18 years in prison for FTR plus potential civil commitment. The court determined this punishment was disproportionate relative to the original offense, particularly given that Whitsett's underlying conduct would not have been handled so severely under German law.[8]

**Arizona registrant (Germany, temporary grant).** An RSO facing 15 to 45 years for FTR and threatened with ammonia aversion therapy received a temporary asylum grant because the court classified the aversion therapy as torture. Not a permanent resolution.

**Another German case (denied).** An RSO facing up to six years for a probation violation — failing to allow a probation officer to access electronic devices and possessing sex toys — was denied asylum. The German court did not find these circumstances sufficient.

**Germany's February 2024 policy change.** According to Steven Whitsett, Germany revised its official policy in February 2024 to automatically exclude people convicted of sex crimes from the asylum process. They are directed instead to pursue work visas through normal immigration channels.[9]

What the cases actually establish: Asylum for U.S. RSOs is not a strategy — it is a legal outcome that has occurred in a handful of specific cases where the facts aligned with a very narrow legal standard. The common thread in the successful cases is not the registry itself. It is the punishment for FTR being so extreme — years or decades in prison, civil commitment — that a foreign court found extradition would amount to persecution. In most FTR cases, the punishment does not reach that threshold, and German courts said so directly. The conviction type and the conduct involved also matter: the Canadian case turned on the conduct not being a crime in Canada. An RSO whose underlying offense would be prosecuted similarly in the destination country has no basis for a successful asylum claim.

Asylum requires a person to be in the destination country before applying — which means successfully entering it first, clearing whatever entry barriers exist, and then navigating a lengthy legal process with no guarantee of success and no U.S. welfare support during the wait. Even if successful, the person is a wanted individual under U.S. law, permanently exiled, unable to visit family in the U.S. without immediate arrest.

## The Legal Pathway: Work Visa to Residency

The only reproducible pathway to legal long-term emigration is through the immigration system: obtain legal status in the destination country, establish residency, eventually pursue permanent residency or naturalization if eligible. The most common entry point is a work visa.

Work visa applications subject applicants to the same scrutiny as any long-term immigration application. For most countries, this means a criminal record check. For RSOs with offenses against minors, countries that impose categorical bars at the immigration stage — which include most of the English-speaking world and many others — will deny the visa application at this point even if tourist entry was theoretically possible.

Germany's current position illustrates the dynamic: after closing the asylum pathway for RSOs in 2024, German immigration directed RSOs to pursue work visas. A work visa application will surface the conviction. If Germany's immigration rules disqualify the applicant on that basis, the work visa is denied. The asylum door is closed; the work visa door depends on the conviction.

For a person with specialized skills, existing professional relationships in a destination country, or significant capital, this path is real. For most people, the barrier at the work visa stage is no different from the barrier at any other stage: the criminal record.

Retirement and passive income visa programs — which exist in a growing number of countries — screen criminal records to varying degrees, as described in the section above. Portugal's D7 allows minor offenses below a one-year threshold; Mexico is discretionary; Panama categorically bars felons. A person with sufficient income who cannot qualify for employment-based immigration should verify the specific criminal record rules of any retirement visa program before assuming it is accessible. Programs change frequently; direct inquiry with the destination country's immigration authority is required.

## The Honest Assessment

In 2016, the New York Division of Criminal Justice Services found 165 RSOs out of 39,377 — approximately 0.4% — residing outside the United States. Twenty-eight of those were classified as Level III, the highest risk tier.[10] The number is small. It is not zero. Emigration is not impossible — it is difficult, legally demanding, and dependent on a combination of factors that most people do not have.

The person who has a realistic emigration option tends to look something like this: sufficient retirement income or in-demand professional skills; a specific destination country whose immigration rules allow for the specific conviction type; existing ties to the destination (a foreign spouse or partner, prior work history there, language fluency); and a conviction that does not trigger the destination country's categorical immigration bars. When those factors align, legal emigration is worth serious research and professional immigration counsel.

For the majority of people who do not have that combination, the energy spent researching emigration is often better spent on the domestic strategies covered in Chapters 5, 6, and 11 through 13. The domestic landscape — while genuinely difficult — offers more realistic pathways for more people than emigration does.

If emigration is the goal, the path is legal entry through the front door, not flight through a side exit. The side exit leads to fugitive status, restricted movement, permanent separation from family, and a federal charge that does not expire. The front door is hard to get through. But it is the only door that leads anywhere worth going.
