# NO CLEAN SLATE
## Chapter 1: Felony vs. RSO — Two Different Sets of Rules

---

Before you book a flight, sign a lease, or cross a state line, you need to understand something that most people coming out of the system never get explained clearly: a felony conviction and sex offender registration are not the same thing. They are not variations of the same problem. They are governed by entirely different legal frameworks, enforced by different mechanisms, and they follow you in different ways.

Mixing them up leads to bad decisions. So does assuming that because one applies to you, the other doesn't matter — or vice versa. This chapter draws the line cleanly between the two, explains what each one actually does to your freedom of movement, and sets up the vocabulary the rest of this book runs on.

---

## The Two Systems

### A Felony Conviction

A felony conviction is the product of a criminal proceeding. A court found you guilty of a crime serious enough to warrant more than a year of potential imprisonment, and that judgment is now part of your permanent public record. Once your sentence is served — prison, parole, probation, supervised release — the state's formal leverage over you largely ends.

What remains are *collateral consequences*: the downstream legal, civil, and practical effects of carrying that conviction on your record. These are not part of your sentence. No judge announces them from the bench. They accumulate in statutes, administrative codes, private policies, and institutional rules. A felony conviction can bar you from certain professional licenses, restrict access to federal housing programs, prohibit you from owning a firearm, and complicate international travel. But in most cases, once you are off paper — no longer under any form of supervision — you are a free person subject to those collateral consequences and nothing more.

The key characteristic of a felony record: it is mostly a *passive* problem. It sits there. It surfaces when someone runs a background check, when you apply for a visa, when you cross a border whose government has reason to care. You do not have to report to anyone. You do not have to notify the government before you move. You do not have to check in with a state agency when you travel. The record follows you, but it does not require ongoing action from you.

### Sex Offender Registration

Sex offender registration is a different animal entirely. Legally, it is classified as a civil regulatory scheme, not a criminal punishment. The U.S. Supreme Court established that framing in *Smith v. Doe* (2003), ruling 6–3 that registration is a civil regulatory measure — not punishment — and can be applied retroactively without violating the Ex Post Facto Clause.[2] That holding remains controlling federal precedent. Courts at the state and lower federal level, however, have increasingly used the same *Smith* framework to strike down registration schemes that have grown so restrictive in practice that their effects cross into punishment — most significantly in Michigan and Tennessee.[3] The label "civil" has limits. But the baseline rule stands: registration is not classified as punishment, and that classification drives everything that follows. The practical effect: registration obligations do not expire when your criminal sentence ends. They are a separate, ongoing legal requirement imposed on top of whatever sentence you served.

Registration requires *active compliance*. You must report to a law enforcement agency on a schedule. You must notify the state when you change your address, your employment, your vehicle, or your online identifiers. In most states, you must give advance notice before traveling internationally. If you move to a new state, you must register there — often within a matter of days. Miss a required check-in, and you have committed a new felony. Cross the wrong line without notifying the right people, and that felony becomes federal.

The key characteristic of RSO registration: it is an *active* problem requiring constant, ongoing compliance. The record follows you, and it demands things of you continuously.

## What Happens When Both Apply

Some people carry both. A sex offense conviction results in both a felony record and registration requirements. When that is the case, both frameworks apply — simultaneously and independently.

This is not a niche situation. A significant portion of people navigating these issues carry both designations. The felony record creates one set of barriers. The registration obligation creates a separate, usually more demanding set of barriers. Neither cancels out the other. Neither excuse from compliance with the other.

If you are in this situation, you need to track both systems and understand where they interact and where they diverge. Some countries that bar felons from entry also independently bar registered persons. Some states that have restrictive policies toward felons have separate and additional restrictions on registered persons. The burdens stack.

---

## Where Supervision Fits In

If you are still on parole, probation, or supervised release — what people in the system call being *on paper* — a third framework sits on top of both of the above. Your supervision conditions are a specific set of rules attached to your individual case by a court or agency. They can restrict travel, require approval before changing residence, prohibit contact with certain people or places, and limit your activities in ways that go far beyond what the law requires of anyone else with your record.

This book is written primarily for people who are *off paper* — past the point of formal supervision. But supervision conditions are referenced throughout because many readers will know someone on paper, because understanding the contrast matters, and because some chapters deal with interstate transfers and related processes that touch supervised individuals directly.

Chapter 2 covers supervision in depth. For now, the point is simply this: if you are still on paper, everything in this book has to be filtered through your supervision conditions first. Your PO has authority that supersedes the general rules described here.

---

## The Practical Difference in Plain Terms

Here is the sharpest way to state the difference between the two systems:

A person with a felony record, once off paper, can move to another state without notifying any government agency. They can travel internationally if they have a valid passport and the destination country admits them. The record may create problems at the border, in a visa application, or on a background check — but those problems arise because of what others see, not because of any legal obligation the person failed to meet.

A registered person, whether off paper or not, cannot move to another state without registering in the new jurisdiction — often within three to five business days. They cannot travel internationally without notifying their state registry office at least 21 days in advance in most states. If they overstay a hotel in a state with narrow travel windows, they may have technically established a registration requirement they did not know about. The law follows them and demands things from them constantly.

The felony record is a wall with doors in it. Some doors are locked, some are not, and the landscape of which is which changes depending on where you are and where you are going.

RSO registration is a leash — a federal and state-regulated leash with length and tension that varies by state, by tier, and by the nature of the underlying offense.

Both can be navigated. Neither is navigable without understanding what it is.

## Key Terms: Sidebar Reference

The following terms are used throughout this book. They are defined here once, plainly, so the reader does not have to wonder what they mean in later chapters.

---

> ### GLOSSARY: TERMS USED IN THIS BOOK
>
> **Adam Walsh Act (AWA)**
> Federal law passed in 2006 that established the national framework for sex offender registration. SORNA is the registration section of the AWA. As of September 2024, only 18 states, 4 territories, and 137 tribal nations are considered "substantially compliant" with the AWA.[1]
>
> **Angel Watch Center (AWC)**
> A unit within the Department of Homeland Security that coordinates with Interpol and foreign governments to notify destination countries when a covered sex offender (one with a conviction involving a minor) has filed travel notice. The AWC also has authority to flag registered persons before they board international flights.
>
> **Collateral Consequences**
> The legal, civil, and practical penalties that attach to a criminal conviction *outside* of the formal sentence. These include loss of firearms rights, professional licensing restrictions, immigration consequences, and restrictions on public benefits. They are not pronounced at sentencing — they exist in statutes and policies and surface later.
>
> **Conviction**
> A formal legal finding of guilt by a court. The starting point for all collateral consequences. Arrests, charges, and pleas that were dismissed or reduced do not constitute convictions, though they may still appear on background checks.
>
> **Covered Sex Offender**
> Under International Megan's Law, a specific legal designation for registered persons whose offense involved a minor (anyone under 18) and who are currently required to register. Covered sex offenders are subject to the passport notation requirement.
>
> **Failure to Register (FTR)**
> The crime of not meeting a registration obligation — missing a check-in, failing to update required information, or moving without registering. In most states, FTR is a felony. If crossing a state line is involved, it becomes a federal crime under the Wetterling Act and SORNA.
>
> **International Megan's Law (IML)**
> Federal law passed in 2016 that requires registered persons to give 21 days advance notice before international travel and requires covered sex offenders to carry a passport stamped with a notation identifying them as convicted of a sex offense against a minor.
>
> **Off Paper**
> Slang for having completed all terms of supervision — prison, parole, probation, and supervised release. A person who is off paper is no longer subject to supervision conditions. They remain subject to collateral consequences of their conviction and, if applicable, sex offender registration obligations.
>
> **On Paper**
> Slang for being under any form of active court or agency supervision — parole, probation, supervised release, or a combination. Supervision conditions are set by a court or agency and can restrict movement, residence, employment, and other activities far beyond what law generally requires.
>
> **Registered Person (RP)**
> A person required by law to register as a sex offender. This book uses "registered person" or "RP" rather than "sex offender" as a descriptive term for individuals — consistent with the practice of organizations working in this space.
>
> **Sex Offender Registration and Notification Act (SORNA)**
> The registration section of the Adam Walsh Act. SORNA establishes a three-tier federal classification system based on offense type and sets minimum standards for registration frequency, duration, and information required. States may exceed SORNA's requirements but must meet its minimums to be considered substantially compliant.[4]
>
> **Tier I / Tier II / Tier III**
> The three-level classification system used in SORNA and many state registries. In AWA-compliant states, tier is determined by the offense charged, not by a risk assessment.
> - Tier I: Annual registration for 15 years.
> - Tier II: Registration every 6 months for 25 years.
> - Tier III: Registration every 90 days for life.
> Non-compliant states may use different criteria and different labels (often "Level" rather than "Tier"), and may assign classification through risk assessments rather than offense type.
>
> **Transient Registrant**
> A registered person who lacks a fixed residence. Transient registrants typically face more frequent reporting requirements — in some states, as often as weekly or monthly — and must describe their general location rather than a fixed address.
>
> **Supervised Release**
> A period of federal supervision that follows release from federal prison. Distinct from parole (which applies to state convictions) but functionally similar — the person is subject to conditions set by the court and monitored by a federal probation officer.

## Takeaway

Two records. Two systems. Different rules, different obligations, different consequences for getting it wrong.

A felony conviction mostly creates barriers that you run into — at borders, on applications, in background checks. The record is passive. You navigate around it.

Sex offender registration creates obligations you must continuously fulfill — check-ins, notifications, advance travel notice, registration in every jurisdiction where you establish even a temporary presence. The system is active. You manage it or it manages you.

When both apply, both sets of rules apply. No exceptions, no overlap that cancels one out.

The rest of this book is built on this foundation. Every chapter that follows — whether it covers domestic travel, moving between states, international trips, or expat life — assumes you understand which system governs which problem. Keep this chapter close.
