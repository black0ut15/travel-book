# NO CLEAN SLATE
## Chapter 3: Domestic Travel as a Felon

---

This chapter is the shortest in Part Two, and that is appropriate. Domestic travel as an off-paper felon is largely unrestricted by law. There is no federal statute that prohibits a person with a felony conviction from crossing state lines, booking a flight, or driving from one coast to the other. The constitutional right to travel between states applies to everyone, including people with criminal records.

That said, "largely unrestricted" is not the same as "no issues." There are practical friction points — places where the record surfaces, programs you are disqualified from, and private-sector policies that can complicate plans. This chapter maps all of them so none of them come as a surprise.

The contrast with the next chapter is worth noting upfront. Chapter 4 covers domestic travel for registered persons, who face an entirely different landscape — affirmative registration obligations triggered by the number of days spent in a state, complex residency definitions, and a patchwork of state rules that vary dramatically. None of that applies here. A felon with no registration requirement travels domestically the same way anyone else does, with a few specific exceptions explained below.

---

## The Legal Baseline

Off paper and carrying only a felony record, a person has no legal obligation to notify any government agency before traveling domestically. No advance notice. No check-ins. No registration in the destination state. The record is passive in the domestic travel context — it does not generate obligations, only potential friction.

The exceptions are not travel restrictions. They are pre-existing legal obligations that happen to intersect with travel. An outstanding warrant in any jurisdiction does not become inactive when you cross a state line. Child support arrears can result in passport denial and, in some states, driver's license suspension. An active restraining order defines prohibited locations regardless of what state they are in. None of these are domestic travel laws — they are consequences of unresolved legal matters that follow the person everywhere. Resolve them before they become a problem at a routine traffic stop three states from home.

---

## Flying: What TSA Does and Does Not Do

The Transportation Security Administration's job is to screen passengers and baggage for security threats. It is not a criminal history check agency. TSA does not run felony background checks when a person passes through airport security.[1]

What TSA does check is the No Fly List and the Selectee List, both maintained by the federal Terrorist Screening Center. A felony conviction alone does not place anyone on either list. These lists are terrorism-focused and operated through separate federal authority from the standard criminal history databases. The vast majority of people with felony records are not on them and will not be flagged by them.

The REAL ID requirement is the more practical concern. As of May 2025, a REAL ID-compliant driver's license, state ID, or passport is required for domestic air travel.[2] REAL ID compliance is based on identity verification and proof of lawful presence — not criminal history. Felons can obtain REAL ID-compliant identification in every state. If the current ID is not REAL ID-compliant, get it updated before booking a flight.

What a felony conviction likely costs is access to the trusted traveler programs: TSA PreCheck, Global Entry, NEXUS, SENTRI, and FAST. All require a background check, and TSA's disqualification framework runs on a tiered system.[3] Certain serious felonies — espionage, treason, terrorism, crimes involving transportation security — result in permanent disqualification regardless of when they occurred. A second tier of felonies, including weapons offenses, fraud, kidnapping, and drug distribution, is disqualifying if the conviction occurred within seven years of the application date or the applicant was released from incarceration within five years. Beyond those two tiers, TSA has broad discretion to deny applicants with extensive criminal histories or any period of incarceration exceeding 365 consecutive days. The practical result for most readers: a significant felony conviction will disqualify them from these programs, either permanently or for the relevant time window. Someone with a decades-old minor conviction may still be eligible and should check the current TSA disqualifying offenses list before assuming otherwise. PreCheck provides expedited screening — no removing shoes, laptops, or liquids. Without it, the standard screening lanes apply. That means longer lines, particularly at busy airports during peak times.

As of February 1, 2026, TSA introduced a paid fallback called TSA ConfirmID for travelers who arrive without REAL ID-compliant identification. The fee is $45, covers a 10-day travel window, and requires up to 30 minutes of additional processing time at the checkpoint.[3] It is a workaround, not a solution — but it exists for people caught without compliant ID in a time crunch.

The practical result: flying domestically is available and legal. It just takes longer than it used to.

## Driving Across State Lines

There is no legal restriction on an off-paper felon driving across state lines. A valid driver's license is required. Some felony convictions — particularly DUI-related offenses — can affect license eligibility or result in suspension in specific states, but that is a licensing issue, not a travel prohibition.

During a traffic stop, a felon has no obligation to volunteer their record. Standard requirements apply: license, registration, proof of insurance. If an officer runs the license or plates, the record will surface in database results. That is not a violation of anything — it is simply how the system works. What matters is that there are no outstanding warrants and no active legal obligations being violated.

On firearms: the federal prohibition under 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(1) applies everywhere in the United States, in every vehicle, crossing every state line. No guns — period. State law does not override this. A state with permissive carry laws still cannot authorize a felon to possess a firearm under federal law. Do not test this in a traffic stop.[4]

One more point on driving: cannabis. It remains federally illegal regardless of state law. Transporting cannabis across state lines — even between two states where it is legal — is a federal offense. A felon carrying cannabis in a vehicle crossing a state line has compounded a federal drug charge on top of an existing record. It is not worth it.

## Hotels, Rental Cars, and Where You Sleep

Standard hotels and motels do not run criminal background checks at check-in. They require a valid government-issued ID and typically a credit card or cash deposit. A felony record does not surface in that transaction and does not legally bar a person from renting a room. Private hotels set their own policies, but the industry norm does not include criminal screening for standard guests.

Rental car companies operate similarly. Major rental agencies — Enterprise, Hertz, Avis, Budget, and others — require a valid driver's license and a credit card. They do not run criminal history checks as part of the rental process. A suspended or revoked license is a different matter; that will surface. The felony record itself will not.

Short-term vacation rental platforms are a different story. Airbnb screens U.S. users against criminal databases through a third-party background check service and applies a tiered removal policy based on offense severity.[5] Violent crimes, sex offenses, terrorism-related convictions, and murder result in permanent removal from the platform. Felony burglary and felony larceny trigger a 14-year ban from the date of conviction. Fraud and property damage offenses result in a 7-year ban. Minor offenses — marijuana possession, disorderly conduct — result in no ban at all. An appeal process exists: users can challenge the accuracy of the background report or provide context about the offense and evidence of rehabilitation. One important caveat: Airbnb's background check vendor, Inflection Risk Solutions, has been sued multiple times for providing inaccurate consumer reports. A ban is not always based on accurate information, and errors can be contested. VRBO applies similar but less publicly documented screening standards. These are private-sector policies, not legal restrictions — but they are enforced and can result in a canceled booking or suspended account. For trips where this matters, booking a standard hotel eliminates the friction entirely.

## What Your Record Looks Like at Points of Contact

A felony conviction from any state is visible nationally. The National Crime Information Center (NCIC) maintains criminal history records accessible to law enforcement across all fifty states and federal agencies. Any law enforcement contact — a traffic stop, a field interview, an incident report — can surface the record in real time. The record does not disappear because the person has crossed a state line or because the conviction occurred in a different jurisdiction.

Outstanding warrants work the same way. A warrant issued in one state is visible to law enforcement in every other state. Whether a state pursues extradition depends on the seriousness of the charge and available resources — many jurisdictions will not extradite for low-level misdemeanors from a distant state — but for serious felonies, extradition is routine. Traveling with an unresolved warrant is a gamble with poor odds. Handle it before it handles you.

Travelers driving in Southwest border states should be aware that U.S. Customs and Border Protection operates interior checkpoints well inside the U.S. border — sometimes 25 to 75 miles from the actual boundary. These checkpoints are staffed by federal agents and focused primarily on immigration status, not criminal records. However, law enforcement contact at a checkpoint is law enforcement contact. The record will surface if agents run identification. This is not a reason to avoid those states; it is a reason to have no outstanding warrants and no contraband in the vehicle.

---

## The Practical Playbook

Before any domestic trip, run through this short list:

Check for outstanding warrants in any jurisdiction where legal matters are unresolved. This is a controllable problem when addressed before travel — an arrest at a routine traffic stop far from home is not. Courts and attorneys can often resolve warrant issues without requiring the person to surrender immediately.

Carry REAL ID-compliant identification. A passport works everywhere. A driver's license must be REAL ID-compliant for air travel as of May 2025. Know which one you have before arriving at an airport.

No firearms in the vehicle. No cannabis across state lines. Both points have been made already. They bear repeating because both are federal offenses and both are encountered at exactly the kind of routine traffic stops that felons are more likely to face secondary scrutiny during.

Use standard hotels and rental cars rather than platform-based short-term rentals where criminal screening is more aggressively applied. This is a practical workaround, not a permanent solution — but it eliminates a source of avoidable complications.

At routine points of contact with law enforcement, do not volunteer information that is not legally required. Answer the questions that are asked. Provide the documents that are legally required. Do not lie — that creates new problems. But there is no obligation to narrate a criminal history to a highway patrol officer during a speeding stop.

---

## Takeaway

Domestic travel as an off-paper felon is not the obstacle it often feels like it should be. The legal landscape is largely open. The friction points are real — trusted traveler programs are off the table, platform-based rentals carry screening risk, and the record is visible at every point of law enforcement contact — but none of them close off domestic travel as a practical matter.

Understand the friction points. Plan around them. Move.
