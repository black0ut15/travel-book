# NO CLEAN SLATE
## Chapter 8: International Travel as a Felon

---

The U.S. government does not prohibit off-paper felons from traveling internationally. There is no federal law restricting a person with a felony conviction from leaving the country once supervision ends. The U.S. side of international travel is largely open.

The complication is at the other end. Border agents work for foreign governments, not the American one. Those governments set their own rules about who can enter their countries, and several important destinations have real barriers for people with felony records. The barriers vary by conviction type, sentence length, and how long ago the sentence was completed. Most of the world is accessible. Some of the most obvious destinations — Canada, Japan, Australia, the United Kingdom — are not, or are significantly complicated.

This chapter maps the landscape. Appendix A at the back of this book contains the full country-by-country matrix. This chapter explains the framework, the specific rules for the countries that matter most, and what to do before and at the border.

---

## Getting a Passport

A U.S. felony conviction, by itself, does not disqualify a person from obtaining a U.S. passport. Off-paper felons apply on the standard DS-11 form, the same as anyone else. The record alone is not a bar.[1]

Specific bars to passport issuance do exist, but they are not about the conviction itself. A person cannot receive a passport if they have outstanding child support arrears above a federal threshold (currently $2,500). Certain federal loan defaults and specific conditions attached to drug trafficking sentences can also prevent issuance. An active arrest warrant is a bar. These are financial and legal obligation issues, not record-based restrictions in the traditional sense.[2]

The passport issued to a felon is a standard U.S. passport — no notation of the conviction, no marking of any kind. This is the baseline distinction from RSOs with covered offenses, who receive a passport stamped with a notation identifying them as convicted of a sex offense against a minor. Felons receive a clean document. What the document does not change is what a foreign border agent can see when they run it through their own systems or ask questions.

## How Countries Screen for Criminal Records

Every country has the sovereign right to determine who enters its territory. That authority extends to the discretion of the border agent at the checkpoint on any given day. Even where no formal bar exists, a border agent can deny entry, and that decision is final in the moment. Understanding how countries exercise that authority requires understanding the two points at which screening happens: before the trip and at the border.

**Before travel — visa applications.** Countries that require a visa from U.S. citizens process a criminal background check as part of that application. Most visa forms ask directly about criminal history. Lying on a visa application is itself a crime and can result in a permanent ban from that country in addition to criminal exposure. If the application asks whether the person has been convicted of a criminal offense, the answer must be honest. The conviction type and sentence length matter — some countries only ask about specific offense categories or convictions above a certain sentence threshold, and the application will specify.

**At the border — entry screening.** Countries that do not require a U.S. citizen to obtain a visa in advance still have the authority to screen at the border and to deny entry. The level of screening varies dramatically. Canada has direct access to U.S. criminal records through shared law enforcement databases — a border agent can pull the conviction history by scanning the passport.[3] Most European countries do not have direct real-time access to U.S. criminal records, but their agents can ask questions and can deny entry based on the answers or on information from other screening systems.

The 9/11 effect is real and ongoing. International information sharing on criminal records expanded significantly after 2001. Countries that previously admitted American visitors with no screening have upgraded their border systems and data-sharing arrangements. The record that would have been invisible at a foreign border in 1995 may be visible today. Do not assume a destination country cannot access a conviction just because it did not ask about it on a visa form.

One important note on expungements: an expungement or record sealing under U.S. law may reduce the visibility of a record in domestic background checks, but it does not bind foreign governments. A foreign immigration official may have access to records through law enforcement databases that are not affected by expungement orders. Be cautious about answering "no criminal record" to foreign immigration authorities when an expungement exists. The question should be researched or confirmed with an attorney before travel to any country with known criminal record screening.

## Countries With Real Barriers

### Canada

Canada is the most significant barrier most U.S. felons will face, and it catches people off guard precisely because it feels like crossing a domestic border. It is not. Canada shares criminal records with the United States through integrated law enforcement databases. A border agent scans the passport, pulls the history, and makes the determination on the spot. People with felony convictions are deemed "criminally inadmissible" under Canadian immigration law and will be turned away.[4]

Three pathways exist for felons who need or want to enter Canada.

The first is a **Temporary Resident Permit (TRP)**. A TRP allows entry for a specific purpose and a limited time period, even for someone who is otherwise inadmissible. Applications require a compelling reason for travel. Business purposes and family emergencies have a better track record than vacation requests. TRPs are entirely discretionary, can be valid for up to three years, and must be applied for in advance — the border is not the place to try to sort this out. Processing can take weeks to months.

The second is **Criminal Rehabilitation**. After five years have elapsed from the completion of the entire sentence — including probation, parole, and payment of fines — a person may apply for Criminal Rehabilitation. If granted, it permanently removes the inadmissibility designation. The application requires demonstrating genuine rehabilitation and low reoffending risk. Processing takes up to 12 months. This is the permanent solution for anyone planning to travel to Canada regularly.

The third is **Deemed Rehabilitation**, which is not something a person applies for but rather a status that may be conferred automatically by a border agent after enough time has passed. Generally, a single non-serious offense with a maximum penalty under Canadian law of less than 10 years may be deemed rehabilitated after 10 years from sentence completion. The critical word is "may" — the determination is made entirely at the border agent's discretion and is not guaranteed. Anyone relying on deemed rehabilitation is gambling on an individual officer's judgment at the moment of crossing. Applying for formal Criminal Rehabilitation is the more reliable path for anyone serious about access to Canada.[5]

### Japan

Japan has among the strictest criminal record entry requirements in the world. The relevant rules are set out in Article 5 of Japan's Immigration Control and Refugee Recognition Act. A person is inadmissible if they have been convicted of any offense punishable by one year or more in prison under Japanese law — this is based on the maximum possible sentence for the offense, not the sentence actually served. A person is also inadmissible for any drug-related conviction regardless of sentence length.[6]

There is no statute of limitations in Japan's immigration rules. A conviction from 30 years ago is treated the same as one from last year. There is no general waiver process equivalent to Canada's TRP or Rehabilitation pathway. Some travelers with serious records have entered Japan by not disclosing convictions on the arrival form, but this carries serious risk: discovery results in denial of entry, the cost of the trip is lost, and a permanent ban may follow. Japan's border agents do not always access U.S. criminal databases in real time, but that can change without notice and is not a basis for planning.

For a felon with a conviction punishable by a year or more, Japan should be considered effectively closed unless an immigration attorney familiar with Japanese entry requirements advises otherwise.

### Australia

Australia requires a visa for most visitors and applies a statutory "character test" that screens applicants with serious criminal records. A conviction resulting in a sentence of 12 months or more — actual sentence, not maximum possible — is typically disqualifying. A pattern of criminal conduct that represents a threat to the Australian community can also result in a failed character test even for shorter sentences.[7]

There is no straightforward waiver pathway for most U.S. felons with serious records. Australia's visa application process is thorough and the Department of Home Affairs applies the character test consistently. A felon with a single conviction resulting in a sentence under 12 months may have better prospects, but the application will still surface the conviction and require disclosure.

### United Kingdom

The UK's entry restrictions are set out in Part 9 of the Immigration Rules. Entry is refused if the person has been convicted of a criminal offense and received a custodial sentence of 12 months or more, regardless of when the conviction occurred. Entry is also refused for non-custodial offenses convicted within the past 12 months.[8]

For most U.S. felons, the practical question is the length of the sentence actually served, not the offense classification. A felony conviction resulting in a 6-month sentence that occurred 15 years ago is a very different situation from a recent conviction resulting in a 2-year sentence. The UK does not require a visa for U.S. tourists, which means screening happens at the border. An agent can deny entry on the spot if they determine the person falls under Part 9 grounds.

### New Zealand

New Zealand applies an eligibility requirement tied to sentence length and recency. A person is not eligible to enter if they were sentenced to imprisonment for 12 months or more within the past 10 years, or if they have ever been sentenced to 5 or more years in prison regardless of when.[9] The 5-year-or-more threshold is permanent and has no time limit.

### The Schengen Area and ETIAS

The 27 nations that make up the Schengen Area — most of Continental Europe — do not require visas for U.S. citizens for tourist visits under 90 days and have historically not systematically screened for felony records. Most U.S. felons can travel to France, Germany, Spain, Italy, and most of the continent without significant barriers under the prior regime.

The European Travel Information and Authorization System (ETIAS) — a pre-travel screening system for visa-exempt visitors to Schengen nations — has been in development for years and has faced repeated delays. As of this writing, ETIAS has not yet launched. The Entry/Exit System (EES), which must be fully operational before ETIAS can take effect, is expected to launch in October 2025. ETIAS cannot begin until six months after EES is running across all Schengen borders, putting the current best estimate for ETIAS at late 2026. Until ETIAS launches, U.S. citizens visiting the Schengen Area for up to 90 days continue to travel without any pre-screening authorization requirement.[10]

When ETIAS does launch, U.S. citizens will need to obtain authorization through an online application before traveling to any Schengen country. The application will include questions about criminal history — specifically covering serious offense categories: terrorism (with a 20-year lookback), sexual exploitation of children, human trafficking, drug trafficking, murder, rape, and convictions linked to organized crime or violent acts. Each application with a flagged offense will be reviewed individually. Denial is not automatic even for listed offense types — the system is designed to identify genuine security threats rather than impose categorical bars based on criminal history. Travelers with minor convictions are expected to be approved. Those with serious convictions in the flagged categories will face additional scrutiny and an individual review process.

For felons with convictions outside those specific categories — fraud, theft, assault, drug possession, and most other offenses — ETIAS is unlikely to be a barrier. For those with convictions in the flagged categories, the Schengen Area will require advance planning once the system is operational. Research the current ETIAS status and application requirements before booking any European travel, as implementation details continue to evolve.

### Other Regions

**Mexico** has no formal criminal record bar for U.S. citizens entering as tourists. Discretionary screening at land crossings and airports exists but is not consistently applied. Mexico is accessible for most felons.

**The Caribbean** is generally open. Most island nations allow visitors who have completed their sentences without restriction. Jamaica specifically excludes felons from entry but in practice does not enforce the restriction if five or more years have passed since the conviction. Verify directly with any specific island destination, as policies vary.[11]

**Latin America** broadly allows visitors with criminal records. Bolivia and Paraguay require advance visas where the criminal history question may arise on the application, but most of the region does not impose a formal felony bar.

**Africa** generally does not restrict entry for U.S. citizens with criminal records. Countries requiring advance visas may ask about criminal history on the application. Check visa requirements for the specific destination.

**Most of Asia outside Japan** is variable. India requires an electronic visa that asks about criminal history; the application is reviewed individually. Taiwan and Malaysia can bar visitors with criminal records. Southeast Asian countries — Thailand, Vietnam, Cambodia, Indonesia, Singapore — are generally accessible for tourist visits, though each country's specific rules should be verified. Israel has broad theoretical authority to deny entry to anyone with a criminal record but in practice focuses primarily on security and terrorism concerns.

## Researching a Destination

The only authoritative source for a specific country's current entry requirements is that country's embassy or consulate in the United States. Not a travel blog. Not a forum post. Not this book. Rules change. Enforcement practices change. The embassy gives the current official answer.

The U.S. State Department's country information pages at travel.state.gov provide general entry requirement information for each country, including whether a visa is required and any known special restrictions. They are a useful starting point but do not always specify criminal record screening rules explicitly.

The practical pre-trip checklist: confirm whether a visa is required; if yes, review the visa application for criminal history questions; determine whether the destination has a known bar for felony convictions based on sentence length or offense type; and verify with the embassy if any doubt remains.

**The conviction type and sentence length matter more than the label "felony."** Many countries screen on sentence length — 12 months is the most common threshold — rather than on the U.S. classification of the offense as a felony. A misdemeanor conviction with a 14-month sentence may be treated more harshly than a felony conviction with a 90-day sentence in several countries. Know the sentence, not just the offense classification.

When the trip is important and the destination has known restrictions for the specific conviction type, a brief consultation with an immigration attorney familiar with that country's rules is money well spent. An attorney cannot guarantee entry, but they can give a reliable risk assessment before money is spent on flights and hotels.

## At the Border

Every border agent has discretionary authority. Even where no formal bar exists, an agent can deny entry. That decision is final in the moment — there is no appeal process at the checkpoint.

If asked directly about a criminal record, honesty is the only viable approach. Lying on entry documents or to border agents carries criminal exposure in the destination country and can result in a permanent ban. If the conviction is a bar, it is better to know at the planning stage than at the border. The planning stage is when the research happens, not the departure lounge.

U.S. citizens have an absolute right to reenter the United States regardless of criminal history. No foreign government can deny a U.S. citizen's return to the United States. A denial at a foreign border means a return trip and a lost vacation — not an international incident, not deportation to a third country. This is not a reason to take risks at borders; it is context for understanding the actual worst-case scenario.

## The Access Map

**Generally open:** Continental Europe for tourist visits (with ETIAS caveat for specific offense types), Mexico, most of the Caribbean (Jamaica with caveat), most of Latin America, most of Africa, Israel for non-terrorism matters, Southeast Asia for tourist visits, most of South America.

**Hard barriers or significant screening:** Canada (hard bar with pathways requiring advance planning), Japan (hard bar, minimal workaround), Australia (hard bar for 12+ month sentences), United Kingdom (hard bar for 12+ month sentences), New Zealand (bar for 12+ month sentences within 10 years, or 5+ year sentences at any time).

**Variable — research required:** India, China, Taiwan, Malaysia, Kenya, Iran, and other countries requiring advance visas with criminal history questions. Verify current requirements with the specific country's embassy before booking.

---

## Takeaway

International travel as a felon is available — most of the world is accessible once supervision ends. The barriers are concentrated in specific countries with well-documented rules, primarily Canada, Japan, Australia, and the United Kingdom. The key variables are conviction type and sentence length, not the felony label itself.

Research the destination before booking. Contact the embassy for the definitive answer. Do not lie on entry documents. For countries where barriers exist and the trip matters, a waiver process or advance application — or an immigration attorney — is the path forward.
