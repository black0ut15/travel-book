# NO CLEAN SLATE
## Chapter 6: Moving to a New State — Registered Sex Offenders

---

Chapter 5 covered moving to a new state as a felon. The short version: pack the truck and go, but do your homework on the legal landscape first. There is no notification requirement, no permission to seek, no agency to update.

That changes completely here. Moving to a new state as a registered person is a legal event. It triggers mandatory notifications with specific deadlines. It subjects the conviction to reclassification under a new state's rules, sometimes with consequences that cannot be undone. It requires finding housing that is legally available under residency restrictions before the move, not after. And if done wrong — if even one state is not notified on time — it generates an FTR charge on top of everything else.

This chapter maps the full process. Read it before signing anything.

---

## The Dual Notification Requirement

The single most common cause of FTR charges in an interstate move is notifying only one state. An RP moving from Ohio to Florida must notify Ohio within 72 hours of the move and notify Florida within 48 hours of arrival. Both. Separately. With separate deadlines.[1]

Neither state assumes the other has been told. The systems do not communicate automatically. The RP is responsible for both notifications, and the failure to complete either one can result in a felony charge. If the move crosses a state line, failure to register in the receiving state also triggers federal jurisdiction under the Sex Offender Registration and Notification Act.

Notification to the sending state typically happens before departure. Some states require advance notice — Mississippi requires notification to law enforcement 10 days before moving into the state.[2] The general rule is to contact the home state registry office as early as possible in the planning process, provide the intended move date and the destination address, and document the notification. Get confirmation in writing. The same principle from Chapter 4 applies here: if something goes wrong, the documented notification is the only protection.

Notification to the receiving state happens upon arrival, within whatever registration window that state allows. The receiving state registers the person as a new resident — not as a transfer from the prior state. That means the full initial registration process: valid identification, criminal background check, registry forms, photograph, fingerprinting, and the physical address of the new residence. Some states require DNA collection at the registrant's expense. Registration fees apply in states that charge them.

The receiving state's registration office is the authoritative source on its specific requirements and deadlines. Call before the move. Confirm the deadline for initial registration, what documentation is required, and where to report. Bring everything. The process cannot be completed partially and finished later.

## How Tier Classification Changes Across State Lines

Registration status does not transfer from one state to another. The receiving state applies its own classification rules to the underlying conviction. The result may be the same tier. It may not be.

AWA-compliant states classify registrants by offense type — the tier is determined by what the person was convicted of, not by a risk assessment. Non-AWA-compliant states may use risk-based classification schemes, subjecting the person to a battery of psychological evaluations and actuarial tools before assigning a tier. Moving from a compliant to a non-compliant state, or vice versa, can change the classification and the obligations that come with it.

The practical rule is what the YLOTL source material describes as the law of gravity: status can go up but rarely comes down.[3] Two documented examples illustrate the range of outcomes.

A Tier I registrant finished Ohio's 10-year registration requirement. By Ohio's rules, he was done. He moved to Florida, where every registrant — regardless of tier, regardless of offense, regardless of when the conviction occurred — registers for life. He is now a lifetime registrant and will remain on the Florida registry after his death.

A Tier I registrant moved from Wisconsin to a state that classified him as Tier III. He later moved back to Wisconsin. Wisconsin reclassified him as Tier III based on the receiving state's prior classification, despite his original Tier I designation under Wisconsin law.

These are not edge cases. They are predictable outcomes of a system where classification rules vary dramatically by state and where the worst outcome of any move tends to stick.

States handle out-of-state convictions in one of three ways.[4] The first group — including Arizona, Colorado, Florida, Illinois, Massachusetts, Nevada, Ohio, Texas, and others — requires registration if the offense is comparable to a registrable offense under the new state's law. The second group — including Alabama, Arkansas, Connecticut, Georgia, Oregon, Virginia, and others — requires registration if the conviction jurisdiction required registration, even if the offense would not be registrable if committed in the new state. The third group — including California, Kentucky, New York, and Wyoming — conducts a case-by-case review.

The timed-out trap deserves specific attention. Finishing a registration period in one state does not create any protection in another. A person who has completed the full registration term in State A and is no longer required to register there may still be required to register upon moving to State B. The new state applies its own rules to the conviction, and those rules may require registration even when the person is effectively done in their prior state. Five states — Indiana, Kansas, Minnesota, New Mexico, and Utah — apply whichever state requires the longer registration period, meaning moving to any of these states from a state with a shorter required period automatically extends the registration obligation.[5]

Before choosing a destination state, research how that specific state handles the specific conviction type for out-of-state registrants. The CCRC's 50-state comparison and the YLOTL state appendix are starting points, but the destination state's registry office is the authoritative source on how it will classify the specific offense.

## Residency Restrictions: Where Housing Is Actually Available

Tier classification determines the obligations. Residency restrictions determine whether an address is even legally available. For many RSOs, residency restrictions are the binding constraint on where they can realistically live — not the cost, not the commute, not the school district. The restrictions.

The variation across states is extreme. Some states have no statewide residency restriction. Others prohibit registered persons from living within 1,000, 1,500, or 2,500 feet of schools, parks, daycares, playgrounds, bus stops, and other designated locations. Florida's statewide restriction is 1,000 feet, but Miami-Dade County has a 2,500-foot restriction. The municipal layer matters as much as the state layer.

Municipal ordinances can exist even in states with no statewide restriction. Texas had no statewide residency restriction law as of this writing, but 412 of its municipalities — including 230 of its 353 "home rule" cities with populations over 5,000 — had their own ordinances in effect as of August 2025.[6] A person who moves to Texas based on the absence of a state restriction and lands in a municipality with a 1,000-foot local ordinance has solved nothing.

The geography problem is not hypothetical. In dense urban areas with strict restrictions, the overlapping exclusion zones can mathematically eliminate most or all legal housing. Oklahoma's 2,000-foot restriction from schools and other designated locations eliminated 84% of potential housing in Oklahoma City and 94% in Tulsa when the law was implemented.[7] These are not statistics about inconvenience — they describe situations in which legal housing does not exist in most of a city.

Some states include a grandfather clause protecting residents who established housing before a new restriction was passed or before a school or daycare moved within the restricted zone. Others do not. Know whether the destination state's restriction applies retroactively to existing residents before assuming current housing is safe long-term.

The only way to verify whether a specific address in a specific jurisdiction is legally available is to check against the current residency restriction rules of both the state and the municipality. The destination county's registry office is the starting point. The CCRC's state profiles and state-specific registry offices provide the rules, but a specific address requires checking the specific exclusion zones around it. Some advocates suggest mapping the potential address against local school and park locations before finalizing any housing commitment.

HUD-assisted housing carries an additional categorical bar that follows the person across every state line: those subject to a lifetime sex offender registration requirement are automatically excluded from Section 8 and all HUD-assisted housing under 42 U.S.C. § 13663.[8] This is not a PHA policy — it is a federal statutory prohibition. No PHA discretion applies. If the registration obligation is lifetime, public housing is not available.

## What Makes a State More or Less Restrictive

There is no single "best" or "worst" state for registered persons — the answer depends on the specific conviction, the tier, the employment situation, and what aspects of daily life matter most. But the variables that determine restrictiveness are identifiable, and evaluating a destination state requires running through them.

**Registration duration** is the first variable. Fifteen states require lifetime registration for all registrants regardless of tier: Alabama, Arizona, California, Colorado, Florida, Georgia, Hawaii, Idaho, Mississippi, Montana, New Jersey, Oregon, South Carolina, Tennessee, and Wyoming.[9] In these states, a Tier I conviction with a 15-year federal standard becomes a lifetime obligation. Some of these states allow petition for removal from the registry after a minimum period — but removal is not guaranteed and is not available to everyone.

**Residency restriction severity** is the second. The distance, the categories of excluded locations, and whether municipalities can add their own restrictions on top of state law all matter. A state with no statewide restriction but hundreds of municipal ordinances provides less certainty than a state with a moderate statewide restriction and preemption of local additions.

**Driver's license marking** is a daily practical reality that affects every interaction requiring identification. Some states require the driver's license to carry a notation identifying the holder as a registered person. This creates visible stigma at every hotel check-in, rental car counter, traffic stop, job application, and bar visit. Know whether the destination state marks IDs before moving.

**Employment restrictions** vary by state. Some states bar registered persons from specific licensed professions beyond what general background check practices would affect. These are worth checking against the employment plan before the move.

**Relief availability** — whether a path exists to petition for removal from the registry — may be the most important long-term variable. A state with lifetime registration and no relief pathway is a permanent situation. A state with a 15-year Tier I registration and a viable petition process is a finite one.

**In-person registration frequency** shapes daily life in practical terms. Quarterly check-ins — the Tier III standard — impose a recurring compliance burden that affects employment scheduling, travel, and flexibility. Annual check-ins are materially less intrusive.

The states most consistently identified as most restrictive are Alabama, Florida, Illinois, Louisiana, Oklahoma, and Tennessee. The author of YLOTL describes Alabama and Florida as being at the top of the worst-states list specifically — Alabama for its combination of lifetime registration, residency restrictions, driver's license marking, and near-zero relief options; Florida for the same combination plus aggressive local ordinances that compound the state restrictions.[10]

## Relief: Petitions, Pardons, and the Path Off the Registry

Relief from registration is governed by the state of current residence, not the conviction state. Moving to a new state resets which relief mechanisms are available — and can either open or close pathways depending on where the person lands.

Petition for removal from the registry is available to Tier I registrants — or their equivalent — in 18 states. For out-of-state convictions specifically, 15 states allow petition after a specified number of years from prison release and 3 states start the clock from when residency in that state was established.[11] The states that allow out-of-state convictions to petition include Arkansas, California, Colorado, Georgia, Idaho, Mississippi, Montana, New Jersey, North Carolina, Ohio, Oklahoma, Oregon, Tennessee, Virginia, and Wyoming.

The standard criteria for petition approval are consistent across most states: a clean post-release record with no subsequent convictions, completed supervision, no outstanding fines or fees, and typically the completion of an approved treatment program. Most states also require the court to find that the petition is in the interest of public safety — which means the petition needs to affirmatively demonstrate rehabilitation, not just the passage of time.

Moving to a new state can affect petition eligibility. Some states explicitly allow out-of-state and federal convictions to petition; others limit the petition process to convictions within the state. North Carolina, for example, bars all lifetime registrants from early termination, which means a lifetime registrant from another state who moves to North Carolina may find the door closed that was open before the move.

State pardons offer a separate pathway. A pardon removes the registration obligation but does not remove the conviction from the criminal record. For out-of-state and federal convictions, the pardon landscape is narrow: only Alabama, Arkansas, and Texas explicitly offer pardons to convictions from outside their jurisdictions. Maine, Massachusetts, New York, Rhode Island, the U.S. Virgin Islands, Vermont, West Virginia, and Wyoming do not explicitly exclude them. A small number of additional states offer partial restoration of rights — restoring voting rights or firearms rights — without a full pardon.[12]

The CCRC's 50-state comparison of relief from sex offender registration obligations is the most comprehensive current resource for this analysis. It is updated periodically and includes the specific eligibility rules, waiting periods, and restrictions on out-of-state convictions for each state.

## Choosing a Destination State: A Framework

The right destination depends on the specific conviction, the tier it will receive in the destination state, the employment situation, and the housing geography. No state is universally better. But the differences are concrete enough to matter significantly.

Run through these questions before choosing:

Does the destination state use offense-based or risk-based classification, and what tier will the specific conviction receive? Is the registration duration fixed or subject to petition for removal — and does the petition process apply to out-of-state convictions? Does the state have statewide residency restrictions, and if not, do its municipalities have their own? Does the state mark driver's licenses? Are there employment restrictions beyond the general background check landscape? What relief mechanisms exist, and is the specific conviction eligible?

To make the contrast concrete: Nevada uses the standard AWA offense-based tier system with a defined registration period and allows out-of-state Tier I convictions to petition for removal after 15 years.[13] The state has no statewide residency restriction for most registrants. The AWA compliance means the tier classification is predictable. The housing geography is relatively open compared to states with broad exclusion zones.

Alabama requires lifetime registration for every registrant, regardless of offense type or tier.[14] It has active residency restrictions, marks driver's licenses, has limited employment flexibility, and offers among the narrowest relief options in the country. A 2024 law has further limited the path to pardons for many conviction types.

For a Tier I registrant with a 15-year federal standard, the difference between these two states is the difference between a finite obligation with a possible exit and a permanent one with none. That is not a marginal consideration.

---

## Takeaway

Moving to a new state as a registered person is not a logistical exercise — it is a legal one. The dual notification requirement, the tier reclassification risk, the residency restriction geography, and the effect on relief eligibility all require active research and planning before the move, not after.

Notify both states. Research the receiving state's classification of the specific conviction before committing to the move. Map the residency restrictions against actual housing options in the target area. Evaluate relief mechanisms before moving somewhere that forecloses them.

The wrong move can permanently worsen the registration situation. The right one can meaningfully improve it.
