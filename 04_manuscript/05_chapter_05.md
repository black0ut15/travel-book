# NO CLEAN SLATE
## Chapter 5: Moving to a New State — Felons

---

A felon off paper can move to a new state without notifying any government agency. There is no registry to update, no transfer request to file, no approval to wait for. Pack the truck and go.

What does not go away is the record. And what changes with the new address is the legal landscape that record operates in. Collateral consequences — the downstream legal and practical effects of a felony conviction — are almost entirely governed by state law, and they vary significantly from state to state. Voting rights, firearms access, professional licensing, employment protections, and housing rules all differ depending on where a person lives. Moving without understanding those differences is moving blind.

This chapter gives felons the framework to move strategically. The conviction is fixed. The consequences of it are not — at least not entirely. Where a person chooses to live shapes how much of their life they can rebuild.

---

## The Record Follows You

Moving to a new state does not change what happened in the last one. The original conviction state's record is permanently held in the National Crime Information Center (NCIC), which is accessible to law enforcement and to background check services nationwide. A background check run in Oregon surfaces a conviction from Georgia. A licensing board in Nevada can see the same record as one in New Jersey. The geography of the new address is irrelevant to the geography of the conviction.

What does change across state lines is the set of rules that attach to the record — what rights are lost, what can be restored, what questions employers can ask and when, what housing programs allow, and what pathways exist to reduce the record's long-term footprint. These rules are entirely a function of state law, and they differ enough that the choice of state genuinely matters.

---

## Voting Rights

Voting rights after a felony conviction are governed entirely by state law. There is no federal prohibition on felons voting — the federal government leaves this entirely to the states, and the states do not agree with each other.[1]

Most states restore voting rights either automatically upon release from prison or upon completion of the full sentence including probation and parole. A smaller number go further, restoring rights even while the person is incarcerated or on supervision. Four jurisdictions — the District of Columbia, Maine, Puerto Rico, and Vermont — impose no voting restrictions at all for people with criminal convictions.

At the other end of the spectrum, a handful of states impose broader or longer-lasting restrictions. Florida restores voting rights after full sentence completion for most felonies, but permanently excludes those convicted of murder or felony sex offenses — meaning most RSOs in Florida cannot vote at all, regardless of how long ago the offense occurred.[2] Alabama disenfranchises all registered persons, with a 2024 law making most ineligible for pardons as well. Wyoming restores rights only for first-time non-violent offenses.

Two states are worth flagging as active examples of how quickly this landscape shifts. Virginia's outgoing governor, Glenn Youngkin, rolled back automatic voting rights restoration that his three predecessors had implemented, replacing it with a case-by-case review system that has left more than 300,000 Virginians unable to vote. Incoming Governor Spanberger is expected to reverse course, and a constitutional amendment for automatic restoration is on track for Virginia's 2026 ballot — but as of this writing, Virginia requires individual gubernatorial approval, and the rules are contested.[2] Nebraska passed a voting rights restoration law in 2024, then watched the state Attorney General challenge it as unconstitutional and the Secretary of State stop registering people with felony convictions, before the Nebraska Supreme Court ordered immediate implementation. The law is now in effect — but the episode illustrates how quickly state-level rules can be disrupted by executive or legal action.

The practical trap: a felon who moves to a new state and votes without understanding that state's specific restoration rules is gambling with a new criminal charge. Several Florida residents were arrested in 2022 after being misled by government officials into believing their rights had been restored when they had not. Ignorance of the restoration rules is not a defense.

Before registering to vote in any new state, verify the specific requirements. Many states require only that the sentence be complete. Some require a waiting period after supervision ends. Some require a formal application. The Collateral Consequences Resource Center maintains a current 50-state comparison of civil rights restoration that is the most reliable single reference for this analysis.[3]

## Firearms

The federal baseline is immovable: 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(1) prohibits any person convicted of a crime punishable by imprisonment for more than one year from possessing firearms anywhere in the United States.[4] This applies in every state, regardless of what state law says.

But several states have created pathways — through pardons, expungements, or specific rights restoration procedures — that can, under certain circumstances, lift the federal prohibition as well. The legal mechanism is narrow: if a state restores a person's civil rights in a way that the conviction no longer qualifies as a disqualifying offense under the federal statute, the federal prohibition may not apply. The Supreme Court addressed this in *Caron v. United States* (1998), and subsequent circuit court interpretation has shaped how broadly state restorations are recognized at the federal level.[5]

The practical reality: this is one of the most legally complex areas of post-conviction law, and getting it wrong is a federal crime. A person who believes a state-level expungement, pardon, or rights restoration has removed their firearms disability should have that specific analysis done by a firearms attorney in their state before touching a gun. Not a general criminal defense attorney. A firearms attorney. The CCRC's 50-state chart on firearms rights restoration is the starting point for research, but it is not a substitute for legal advice on this question.[6]

## Professional Licensing

Most professional licenses are issued at the state level, and most states have their own rules about whether a felony conviction disqualifies an applicant. Moving to a new state does not carry over a license — the person starts fresh under the new state's rules. Whether those rules are better or worse than the prior state's depends entirely on the license, the conviction, and the state.

The range is significant. Some states impose categorical bars — a specific conviction type automatically disqualifies an applicant for a specific license, full stop. Others now require licensing boards to conduct an individualized assessment, weighing the nature of the offense, the time elapsed, evidence of rehabilitation, and the relationship between the offense and the licensed profession. A growing number of states have passed occupational licensing reform laws in recent years that shift toward the individualized model. Since 2017, 44 states and the District of Columbia have enacted 86 separate laws limiting state power to deny workplace opportunities based on criminal history. Licensing boards in 20 states are now generally barred from denying licenses unless the applicant's conviction is directly related to the licensed work. In 21 states, applicants can petition a licensing board before enrolling in any required training to get a preliminary determination on whether their record would be disqualifying — a significant practical protection that avoids investing years and money in training only to be denied at the end.[7] Recent major reforms include South Dakota, Nebraska, and Colorado in 2024, and Alabama in 2025, which passed a law prohibiting automatic denial from occupational licensing boards.

The licenses most commonly affected by felony history vary by state but tend to cluster in healthcare (nursing, medical licensing, home health aide), real estate, contracting, cosmetology, education, law, social work, financial services, and security. Some of these are federally regulated at minimum standards; others are entirely state-controlled.

One distinction matters: ban-the-box laws govern employment applications, not licensing. A state with strong fair-chance hiring protections for employers may still allow its licensing boards to deny based on conviction history. These are parallel systems operating under different rules.

The strategic implication for someone choosing a destination state: if a specific professional license is central to the employment plan, the first research step is whether that license is obtainable in the target state with the specific conviction on record. The answer is not always no — but it needs to be known before the move.

## Employment Background Check Laws

Ban-the-box laws delay — not eliminate — the point at which a conviction can be considered in hiring decisions. In states with these laws, employers cannot ask about criminal history on the initial job application. The question gets pushed to a later stage, typically after a conditional offer has been made.

As of 2022, 37 states had adopted ban-the-box laws or policies applicable to public-sector employment. Fifteen states had extended the requirement to private employers as well.[8] Twenty-two major metropolitan areas including New York City, Los Angeles, Chicago, and Seattle had their own local fair-chance hiring ordinances covering private employers within their boundaries.

What ban-the-box does in practice: it gets a felon in the door for an interview before the conviction surfaces. An employer who would have auto-screened an application based on a checkbox now has to make a human assessment after meeting the person. That is not nothing. For people with serious records, it is often the difference between getting a foot in the door and not.

What ban-the-box does not do: it does not prohibit background checks. It does not prevent an employer from declining to hire someone after reviewing their record. It does not apply to federal contractors or to many licensed professions. And it does not apply in states that have not adopted it — in those states, employers can ask about convictions on page one of the application and screen out accordingly.

For a felon evaluating destination states, the presence or absence of meaningful ban-the-box protections for private employers is a material employment consideration. A state like California, Colorado, or New York offers meaningfully different hiring access than a state without these protections.

## Housing

Federal public housing is the area where felons most often encounter a hard wall. Public Housing Authorities — the agencies that administer HUD-assisted housing — have broad discretion to set their own admissions policies for applicants with criminal records.[9] There is no categorical federal law banning felons from public housing. But individual PHAs can and do impose their own bars, and many are highly restrictive.

That discretion has expanded significantly under the current administration. In November 2025, HUD rescinded three major guidance documents that had previously nudged PHAs and federally assisted housing owners toward individualized assessment of criminal records: Notice PIH 2015-19, the 2016 Office of General Counsel guidance, and the 2022 FHEO implementation memo. HUD's November 25, 2025 letter replaced them with a clear directive: safety is paramount, screening is expected, and owners and PHAs should use all available tools to screen applicants. The proposed "Reducing Barriers to HUD-Assisted Housing" rule that would have required individualized assessment was withdrawn in January 2025 before taking effect.

The practical result: PHAs now have active federal encouragement — not restraint — to screen aggressively on criminal history. Access to subsidized housing for felons has become more restricted under current policy than at any point in the past decade. This is a policy position, not a permanent legal rule, and a future administration may reverse it — but the practical landscape as of publication is significantly less favorable than it was under prior guidance.

The one categorical federal housing bar for felons: people subject to lifetime sex offender registration are automatically excluded from Section 8 and other HUD-assisted housing under 42 U.S.C. § 13663.[10] That applies to anyone whose conviction carries a lifetime registration obligation, regardless of which state they move to.

Federal home loan programs — FHA, USDA, and VA — impose no restriction based on criminal history, including felony convictions and sex offenses. A felon who can qualify financially can obtain a federally backed mortgage on the same terms as anyone else. This is worth knowing because it is widely misunderstood.

The private rental market has no uniform rule. Private landlords can screen however they choose. Corporate property management companies typically run comprehensive background checks and apply categorical policies. Individual private landlords vary — some refuse any criminal history, some will consider the circumstances. In a tight rental market, a felon with a serious record will encounter significant friction. In a looser market with more individual landlords, the search is harder but not impossible.

## Expungement and Record Sealing

An expungement or record sealing in the original conviction state does not automatically apply in a new state. A record sealed in Illinois is still visible in NCIC and will still surface in most national background checks. Whether a new state treats an expunged out-of-state record as if it does not exist depends on that state's own laws — and most states have not addressed this clearly.

The growing clean slate movement is worth tracking. Several states have passed automatic expungement laws that clear certain records after a defined waiting period without requiring a petition. Pennsylvania, Michigan, and others have enacted these laws in recent years. For a person choosing a destination state, the availability of automatic or accessible expungement for the specific conviction type is a meaningful long-term factor.

The deeper treatment of expungement, cross-state record recognition, and the paper trail is in Chapter 12. For purposes of moving to a new state, the key point is this: do not assume an expungement in the conviction state provides a clean record in the new state. It may help, but verify before acting on that assumption.

## Choosing a State: Two Contrasting Examples

The right destination state depends on the person's specific situation — conviction type, employment goals, housing needs, and family ties. No state is universally better for felons. But the differences are real and concrete.

Consider two contrasting examples.

**California** has some of the country's most robust fair-chance employment protections. Private employers are prohibited from asking about conviction history before a conditional offer under state law, and the state requires individualized assessment for licensing decisions in many fields. Automatic record sealing is available for many convictions after completion of sentence. Voting rights are restored automatically upon release from state prison — not after completion of parole. The cost of living is high and the rental market is competitive, but the legal landscape for rebuilding a professional life is relatively favorable.

**Alabama**, by contrast, has no statewide ban-the-box law for private employers. Professional licensing boards operate under more categorical rules. Voting rights restoration for felons requires application and approval, not automatic restoration — and a 2024 law has significantly limited that pathway for certain offenses. The cost of living and rental market are more accessible, but the formal employment and civic landscape is considerably more restrictive.

Neither state is the right answer for every person. Someone with construction skills, no licensing ambitions, and family support in Alabama may be better off there than in an expensive California market. Someone with healthcare credentials and a drug conviction may find California's individualized licensing assessment framework opens doors that Alabama's categorical rules close.

The framework is: identify what the specific collateral consequences of the specific conviction are in the target state, map them against the specific employment and housing goals, and make a decision with information rather than assumptions. The Collateral Consequences Resource Center's state profiles are the most comprehensive free resource for this analysis.[11]

---

## Takeaway

Moving states as a felon is legally uncomplicated. There is no form to file, no permission to seek, no registry to update. But the legal landscape waiting in the new state is not neutral — it is shaped by state-specific rules that vary enough to matter significantly.

Before moving, know the voting rights rules in the destination state and what restoration requires. Know whether the target profession requires a license and whether that license is obtainable with the specific conviction on record. Know whether the state's employment protections will get through the door before the conviction gets to the interviewer. Know the housing options. Know the expungement or clean slate pathway.

The record is not going away. But where a person takes it changes what they can do with the rest of their life.
