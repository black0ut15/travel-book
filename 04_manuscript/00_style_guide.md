## CITATION STANDARD: HYBRID

### The Rule in Plain Terms
Name important sources in the prose when they matter. Collect specific citations — statutes, court cases, hard data points — in a numbered endnotes section at the end of each chapter. Readers who want to verify something can find it there. Readers who don't can ignore it.

### When to Cite Inline (Name the Source in the Prose)
Use inline attribution when the source is the point — when *who said it* or *what authority established it* adds weight to the sentence. Examples:

- Federal statutes and regulations: name them in the text.
  - ✅ "Under the Adam Walsh Act..."
  - ✅ "International Megan's Law requires..."
  - ✅ "34 U.S.C. § 21501 mandates..."

- Court decisions: name the case and year in the text when the ruling itself is the point.
  - ✅ "The Supreme Court ruled in *Smith v. Doe* (2003) that..."
  - ✅ "The Sixth Circuit held in *Does v. Snyder* (2016) that..."

- Named government agencies or programs: name them naturally.
  - ✅ "The SMART Office reports..."
  - ✅ "According to the Angel Watch Center..."

- Named authoritative reports: name them if they're well-known or authoritative enough to add credibility.
  - ✅ "The 2022 SMART Office world registry report found..."

### When to Use Endnotes (Numbered Citation at Chapter End)
Use an endnote number [1] in the text and collect the full citation in the chapter's endnotes section when:

- A specific statistic, figure, or data point needs a traceable source
- A direct or near-direct quote from a written source is used
- A legal citation needs its full form (title, section, subsection) but including it inline would clutter the sentence
- A claim could reasonably be disputed and a reader might want to verify it
- A source is important but not well-known enough to name inline without explanation

### What Does NOT Need a Citation
- General legal principles that are well-established and uncontested
- Explanations of how a system works that are drawn from the author's knowledge and experience
- Common knowledge within the subject matter
- Paraphrased general information from multiple converging sources

## ENDNOTES FORMAT

Endnotes appear at the end of each chapter under the heading `## Notes`. They are numbered sequentially within each chapter (Chapter 1 restarts at 1, Chapter 2 restarts at 1, etc.).

### Court Cases
> Last Name v. Last Name, Volume U.S. Page (Year).
> Example: Smith v. Doe, 538 U.S. 84 (2003).

For circuit court decisions:
> Does v. Snyder, 834 F.3d 696 (6th Cir. 2016).

### Federal Statutes
> Short title, U.S.C. Title § Section.
> Example: Sex Offender Registration and Notification Act, 34 U.S.C. § 20901 et seq.

Or by public law number where more useful:
> Adam Walsh Child Protection and Safety Act, Pub. L. 109-248 (2006).

### Government Reports and Publications
> Agency Name, *Title of Report* (Month Year), URL if available.
> Example: SMART Office, *Sex Offender Registration and Notification in the United States* (March 2019), https://smart.ojp.gov.

### Books
> Author Last, First. *Title*. Publisher, Year.
> Example: [Author]. *Your Life on the List, 2025 Edition*. 2025.

### Web Sources
> Organization or Author. "Title of Page." Website Name, Date. URL.

## INLINE MARKER FORMAT

Endnote markers in the text are superscript numbers in brackets: **[1]**

Place the marker:
- After the relevant fact or sentence, before the period when possible
- After a clause, not mid-sentence
- Never inside a glossary sidebar entry (those are reference elements, not running text)

Example running text:
> "As of September 2024, eighteen states, four U.S. territories, and 137 tribal nations had achieved substantial AWA compliance.**[1]**"

## RESEARCH FLAG FORMAT

Active research flags that are unresolved use this format in the draft:
```
> [NEEDS RESEARCH] Description of what is needed and why.
```

Resolved flags are converted to citation notes:
```
> **CITATION NOTE:** [summary of what was verified and when]
```

Resolved flags with a clear citation are removed from the text entirely and their citation moved to the endnotes section.

---

## GLOSSARY SIDEBARS

- Formatted as blockquote sections within the chapter
- No inline citations inside sidebar entries — these are definition blocks, not running argument
- Terms are defined once in Chapter 1; subsequent chapters use the terms without re-defining them unless a chapter-specific nuance warrants a brief clarification

---

## AUTHOR ANECDOTES

- Used selectively, where they illustrate a point that would otherwise be abstract
- Set off with a line break and a brief em-dash lead-in or a natural paragraph transition
- Written in third-person consistent with the rest of the book's voice, or flagged with [AUTHOR ANECDOTE — insert here] as a placeholder for the author to draft in their own words

## CHAPTER FILE STRUCTURE (Standard)

Each chapter file follows this order:

1. Book title and chapter heading
2. Opening (no heading)
3. Body sections (## and ### headings)
4. Glossary sidebar (Chapter 1 only; subsequent chapters as needed)
5. Takeaway section
6. Next chapter line
7. Glossary sidebar callout block (if applicable)
8. `## Notes` section with numbered endnotes
9. Any unresolved `[NEEDS RESEARCH]` flags (below the notes)
