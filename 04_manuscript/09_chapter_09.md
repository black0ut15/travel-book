# NO CLEAN SLATE
## Chapter 9: International Travel as a Registered Sex Offender

---

Chapter 8 covered international travel for felons. The barriers there originate at the destination — countries that screen for criminal records and turn people away at their borders. For registered persons, the dynamic is fundamentally different. The United States government is an active participant in preventing RSO travel, notifying destination countries through a coordinated federal apparatus before the registered person leaves American soil. The barriers do not wait at the other end. They begin before departure.

This chapter explains that apparatus, its reach, and what international travel — if any — remains viable for registered persons.

---

## International Megan's Law: The Framework

International Megan's Law (IML), passed in 2016 as Public Law 114-119, is a U.S. law — not an international treaty. It creates obligations for U.S. registered persons and directs the federal government to notify foreign governments about RSO travel. Its stated purpose is preventing transnational child sexual exploitation. Its practical effect is to make international travel significantly more difficult for any registered person and nearly impossible for those convicted of offenses against minors.[1]

IML operates through two primary mechanisms. The first is a mandatory 21-day advance notice requirement that applies to all registered persons before any international travel. The second is a passport "unique identifier" — a stamp — applied to the passports of "covered sex offenders," a specific legal category within the broader RSO population.

The law built its enforcement on an explicit assumption: that registered persons travel internationally primarily for exploitative purposes. The Angel Watch Center, the federal office that administers IML's notification operations, describes its mission as "targeting individuals who have been previously convicted of sexual crimes against a child and who may pose a potential new threat: traveling overseas for the purpose of sexually abusing or exploiting minors."[2] That assumption is empirically contested. It is nonetheless embedded in the law and drives the enforcement apparatus.

The scale of that apparatus is significant. In roughly two years following IML's implementation, the U.S. government sent notices to foreign countries about 10,541 covered sex offenders. As of July 2020, 3,681 of those individuals had been denied entry into destination nations based on those notices.[3]

---

## The 21-Day Notice: What It Requires

Every registered person — not just covered sex offenders, but all RSOs — must notify their home state registry at least 21 days before any international travel.[4] The requirement is codified at 34 U.S.C. §§ 21501–21510. In October 2020, then-Attorney General Barr extended enforcement of the 21-day rule to non-AWA-compliant states, making the requirement functionally nationwide regardless of whether the home state has codified it into its own law.

The notice is not a brief heads-up. It must include:

Full identifying information — name, aliases, date of birth, sex, citizenship, passport number and issuing country. Complete travel details — destination countries, specific dates and places of departure, arrival, and return; the name of each airport, train station, or port; flight, train, or ship numbers; departure and arrival times; addresses at intermediate stops; destination address or contact information; and purpose of travel (business, military, relocation, or other).

The state registry forwards the notice to federal authorities. From there it routes through the Angel Watch Center to Interpol, which notifies the destination country's relevant agencies. The foreign government typically receives advance notice of the registrant's planned arrival before the registrant boards the plane.

State implementation varies. As of late 2022, 19 states had codified the 21-day requirement into state law and actively forwarded notices to federal authorities; 21 more states were deferring to federal law without having codified it; 13 states had no codified requirement but may still have their own travel notice obligations. New York requires only 10 days. New Hampshire requires notice only for international stays of five or more days at the same address. The Northern Mariana Islands requires only three days. Every other jurisdiction applies 21 days as the standard.[5]

The practical consequences are significant. International travel cannot be booked last-minute. A trip departing on a Saturday requires notice filed no later than the previous Saturday — 21 full days in advance. Any change in itinerary after notice has been submitted requires re-notification. The entire notice must be accurate and complete; an error is not a technicality. Failure to file is not a gray area — it generates federal FTR exposure under SORNA.

---

## The Passport Stamp

"Covered sex offender" is a specific legal category under IML, defined at 22 U.S.C. § 212b(c)(1). A person is a covered sex offender if they meet two conditions simultaneously: they were convicted of a sex offense against a minor, and they are currently required to register as a sex offender. Both conditions must be present.[6]

Covered sex offenders are required to carry passports stamped with the following text: "The bearer was convicted of a sex offense against a minor, and is a covered 'sex offender' pursuant to 22 United States Code Section 212b(c)(l)."

The stamp is not subtle. It is a permanent notation visible to every immigration officer who opens the passport at any border crossing anywhere in the world. Before any question is asked, any form reviewed, or any decision made, the border agent sees the conviction. Countries that received a 21-day IML notice through the AWC pipeline now also have visible confirmation at the moment of arrival.

Covered sex offenders are additionally prohibited from obtaining a passport card — the wallet-sized document used at land border crossings and sea ports of entry. They may carry only the standard passport book. This restriction eliminates the informal flexibility that passport cards provide at land borders with Canada and Mexico.

The stamp applies only to covered sex offenders — those with offenses against minors who are currently required to register. RSOs whose conviction did not involve a minor do not receive the stamp. Their 21-day notice obligation remains, but the visible identification at the border is absent. RSOs who have completed their registration period and are no longer required to register may no longer qualify as covered sex offenders under the statute — the definition links the status to current registration requirement. Courts continue to work through the question of whether SORNA obligations persist for those who have timed out of registration in their home state, but the statutory language of IML itself is tied to current registration.[7]

RSOs who believe they have been incorrectly identified as a covered sex offender should contact the Angel Watch Center directly to challenge the designation.

---

## The Angel Watch Center and HSI Enforcement Abroad

The Angel Watch Center (AWC) is operated by DHS's Immigration and Customs Enforcement. It is the federal clearinghouse that receives 21-day notices from state registries, processes them, and routes them to Interpol for distribution to destination countries. The AWC is the operational core of Operation Angel Watch — IML's active enforcement arm.[8]

The pipeline runs: registrant notifies state registry → state registry forwards to DHS/AWC → AWC routes to Interpol → Interpol notifies destination country's agencies. This sequence typically completes before the registrant's departure date, meaning foreign authorities are waiting with information before the person lands.

The enforcement reach extends beyond the routing function. As of 2024, Homeland Security Investigations (HSI) maintained 93 overseas locations in 56 countries — a global enforcement footprint that has expanded from the 80 offices in 53 countries cited in earlier reporting. HSI agents stationed at foreign airports have intercepted arriving registered persons before they could speak with the destination country's own immigration officers. In documented cases, American federal agents operating on foreign soil have turned U.S. citizens around and put them on return flights — before the destination country acted on its own authority. In one documented incident type, U.S. DHS agents removed RSOs from outbound flights in Florida before departure.[9]

The SMART Office maintains a non-public list of nations that it indicates will reject RSOs. That list is not publicly available. The result is that the RSO community has no official disclosure of which countries are effectively closed to them through the AWC notification system, even when those countries have no formal domestic law barring entry. Two community-maintained travel matrices — at pfr.guide and justfactsnotfear.org — fill part of this gap with volunteer-compiled information, including direct outreach to country representatives. They are not authoritative legal sources but are the most current practical reference available.[10]

## What Countries Are Actually Accessible

The accessible geography for RSOs is substantially narrower than for felons, and it is narrowing. The IML notification system has caused countries without formal entry bars to develop informal denial practices as they receive advance notices. A country that has no law barring RSO entry may still deny entry based on an AWC notice.

Two categories of restriction apply from the source material.

**Countries with documented laws formally barring RSO entry** (as of July 2024, per YLOTL citing the PFR Guide and Just Facts Not Fear matrices): Argentina, Australia, Brazil, Canada, China, Ireland, Japan, Mexico, New Zealand, Panama, South Africa, The Philippines, United Kingdom.[11]

**Countries with documented reports of RSO turnbacks** — based on self-reports from registered persons; these do not confirm a formal law barring entry but indicate a real pattern of denial: Argentina, Australia, Bahamas, Barbados, Belize, Brazil, Cambodia, Canada, Cayman Islands, Chile, China, Colombia, Costa Rica, Dominican Republic, Dubai, Ecuador, El Salvador, Fiji, Greece, Guatemala, Haiti, Honduras, Hong Kong, India, Indonesia, Ireland, Israel, Jamaica, Japan, Kenya, Laos, Malaysia, Mexico, New Zealand, Nicaragua, Panama, Peru, Senegal, Singapore, South Africa, South Korea, Spain, St. Lucia, Taiwan, Thailand, The Philippines, Ukraine, United Kingdom (including Anguilla, Bermuda, British Virgin Islands, Montserrat, Pitcairn Islands, Turks and Caicos), Venezuela, Vietnam.[12]

The overlap between the two lists is telling. Countries that have formal laws barring RSO entry also account for a large portion of the self-reported turnbacks. But the self-reported list extends into countries without formal bars — which reflects the AWC notification system at work. A country receives a notice that an RSO is traveling there; that country's border agents deny entry using whatever discretionary authority they hold, even without a specific statutory prohibition.

**What appears to remain accessible:** The most consistent community reports of successful travel point to parts of sub-Saharan Africa not on the above lists, some Eastern European countries not listed, and a handful of other destinations. The PFR Guide matrix (pfr.guide) maintains an updated sorting that highlights countries where registered persons have reported successful entry. As of July 2025, the site added a sorted view displaying only "green" countries. This resource is maintained by volunteers and reflects community experience, not legal authority. It should be treated as a starting point for research, not a guarantee.

The practical reality: the accessible world for a covered sex offender — someone with an offense against a minor who is currently required to register, carrying a stamped passport — is dramatically smaller than for any other traveler. The geographic narrowing is ongoing. As more nations adopt their own sex offense registries and respond to IML notices, the landscape continues to close.

---

## Countries With Their Own Sex Offense Registries

The push to export the U.S. registry model internationally has been a deliberate policy. ECPAT (formerly End Child Prostitution in Asian Tourism) and the SMART Office have actively worked with foreign governments to establish sex offense registration schemes. As of supplemental research in 2022, approximately 40 nations had adopted at least some form of sex offense registry.[13]

Among them: Albania, Argentina, Australia, Canada, France, Germany, India, Ireland, Jamaica, Japan, Kenya, Malaysia, New Zealand, South Africa, South Korea, Spain, Taiwan, United Kingdom, and others. Several nations have registries that are publicly searchable online; others release registry information under specific circumstances or upon request.

The existence of a foreign registry does not automatically mean an RSO traveling there will be required to register or will be denied entry — rules vary widely. But it signals heightened coordination with U.S. authorities and increased likelihood of scrutiny. Nations actively building registry infrastructure are also actively receiving and acting on IML notices.

Several nations reportedly had registries in development as of the 2022 report: Dominican Republic, Ethiopia, Ghana, Grenada, Namibia, Sierra Leone, Slovenia, Uganda, and Zimbabwe. This list is growing.

Ecuador's Supreme Court ruled that country's sex offense registry unconstitutional — a notable exception to the global trend, though Ecuador's ongoing civil unrest and transnational crime issues make it a complicated destination in any event.

## The Covered Sex Offender Distinction

Not every RSO is a covered sex offender under IML. This distinction matters significantly for international travel, and it is worth restating clearly.

The passport stamp, and some of the most aggressive AWC notification mechanisms, apply specifically to those convicted of a sex offense against a minor who are currently required to register. An RSO whose conviction did not involve a minor does not receive the stamp. The 21-day notice still applies — that obligation covers all registered persons — but the visible identification at every border is absent.

An RSO who has completed a finite registration period and is no longer required to register in their home state may no longer meet the definition of "covered sex offender" under the statute. That person may still be required to file the 21-day notice under SORNA (this is an active legal question in several circuits), but the passport stamp does not apply to someone who is not currently required to register.

The same underlying conviction can therefore produce significantly different international travel experiences depending on whether it involved a minor and whether registration remains current. A Tier I registrant in a state with a 15-year registration period who has timed out faces a different international landscape than a lifetime registrant with a covered offense. The difference is not theoretical — it affects what the passport looks like and how the AWC notification system classifies the travel.

## The Practical Framework

**File the 21-day notice without exception.** The legal obligation applies to all RSOs for all international travel. FTR exposure under federal SORNA is not a proportionate trade-off for any vacation. File accurately and completely, with all itinerary details, as far in advance as the 21-day minimum allows. If the itinerary changes, refile.

**The notice obligation applies even to countries where entry will likely be denied.** The duty to notify is not contingent on the likelihood of successful entry. File the notice, then pursue the trip or not based on the research.

**Use the community travel matrices as a starting point, not an ending point.** The PFR Guide (pfr.guide) and Just Facts Not Fear (justfactsnotfear.org) matrices are the most current practical references available for RSO-specific international travel experience. Both are maintained by advocates with community-sourced data. Neither is a legal authority. Confirm with the specific country's embassy before booking.

**Do not rely on the absence of a formal entry bar as assurance of entry.** The AWC notice system means destination governments receive advance information that can generate denials even without a specific domestic law barring RSO entry. Informal practice and formal law are both operational barriers.

**Understand the passport stamp's practical effect.** For covered sex offenders, every border crossing anywhere in the world involves presenting a passport that announces the conviction before any other interaction. Factor this into planning for any destination, including countries not on the denial list.

**If the covered sex offender designation is incorrect, challenge it.** RSOs who believe they have been wrongly identified as covered sex offenders under IML should contact the Angel Watch Center directly. The AWC contact pathway exists specifically for this purpose.

The full country-by-country RSO travel matrix is in Appendix A alongside the felon matrix.

## Takeaway

International travel as a registered person is subject to a layer of enforcement that has no equivalent for any other traveler: the U.S. government actively notifies destination countries before departure, stamps passports with conviction information for covered sex offenders, and stations agents abroad to intercept arriving RSOs before foreign immigration authorities act. This apparatus was built on the premise that RSOs travel to offend, and it operates on that premise regardless of any individual's actual circumstances or risk level.

The practical result is that accessible international destinations for RSOs — particularly for covered sex offenders with the passport stamp — are significantly narrower than for other travelers, and the list is contracting. Travel is possible for some RSOs to some destinations. It requires the 21-day notice without exception, careful pre-trip research using the best available community resources, direct embassy confirmation, and a clear-eyed understanding of what the passport reveals at every border.
