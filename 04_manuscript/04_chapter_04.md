# NO CLEAN SLATE
## Chapter 4: Domestic Travel as a Registered Sex Offender

---

Chapter 3 established that a felon off paper can cross a state line without notifying anyone. That changes here. A registered person cannot take a week-long road trip, visit family in another state, or even stay overnight in a hotel two counties away without understanding the legal obligations that travel triggers — both from the home state and the destination state. Miss those obligations, and a vacation becomes a Failure to Register charge.

This chapter is not meant to discourage travel. It is meant to make travel survivable. The rules are complex, inconsistent, and in some states aggressively enforced. But they are knowable. The readers who get into trouble are the ones who assume the rules are simpler than they are, or who assume that because they did not intend to break a rule, the rule does not apply. Intention is not a defense to FTR. Ignorance is not a defense to FTR. Compliance is the only defense.

---

## Two Questions Before Every Trip

Every domestic trip requires two compliance questions answered before departure.

The first: what does the home state require before leaving? The second: what does the destination state require upon arrival?

These are separate questions with separate answers, and both need to be resolved before anything gets booked.

Home state obligations vary significantly. Some states require only that the registrant notify the local registry office of travel plans. Some require an itinerary. Illinois requires notification and a travel itinerary for any travel away from home lasting three days or more.[1] Alabama requires a formal travel permission form submitted for approval at least three business days before departure — not just notification, but approval.[2] Other states have no explicit statutory travel notification requirement at all.

That last category is a trap. A state with no explicit notification requirement does not mean law enforcement has no expectation. It means the statute is silent. In practice, local registry offices and sheriff's departments almost universally expect to be notified when a registered person leaves the state, regardless of what the statute says or does not say. An RP discovered during a traffic stop in another state — with no record of notification in their home state system — looks like an absconder. The responding officer will call the home state. If the home state has no record of the person's travel plans, the conversation goes badly.

Notify your home state registry office before any out-of-state travel. Do this even if your state does not explicitly require it. Document the notification — the date, the person you spoke with, what information you provided. If the state allows call recording, record it. If not, take notes. This documentation is protection if the trip goes sideways.

---

## What "Day" Means and Why Getting It Wrong Is Costly

Forty-six states require nonresident registered persons to register with that state once they exceed a threshold number of days in it.[3] Three states — New York, Oregon, and Pennsylvania — did not require visiting RPs to register as of the most recent comprehensive national assessment, though their registry offices still recommend making contact before arrival.

The threshold varies from state to state in ways that are not intuitive. Nevada triggers registration after two days. Alaska allows up to 30 days before registration is required — but still requires a visitor's form submitted before entry. Most states fall somewhere between three and fourteen days.

Two definitions complicate the counting further: what constitutes a "day," and whether days are counted consecutively or in aggregate.

Some states define days as consecutive calendar days from the moment of arrival. Others count aggregate days — meaning the total number of days across all visits in a calendar year, even if those visits are separated by months. Florida and Illinois use aggregate counting, and both trigger registration after just three aggregate days anywhere in the state in a calendar year.[4] A registered person who makes two short visits to Florida — three days in March and two days in September — has not exceeded the threshold on either individual trip. But if they make one more overnight visit before the end of the year, they cross it.

Partial days add another layer. Some states count any portion of a day as a full day. Under that framework, an overnight hotel stay that begins late one evening and ends mid-morning the next day counts as two days, not one. Alabama's statute treats presence for as little as four hours as a day. Florida counts "any part of a day."[5]

The only reliable way to know what the rules are in a specific destination state is to call the registry office in the county or jurisdiction where the stay is planned. Not the state police general line. Not an internet summary. The actual office with jurisdiction over the address where the person will be staying. Ask specifically: how do you count days? Do aggregate visits within the calendar year count? Is a partial day a full day? Get the answer documented.

This is not bureaucratic overcaution. It is the difference between a vacation and a felony charge.

## What Registration in a Destination State Requires

When registration is required in a destination state, every state imposes a minimum set of requirements: valid government-issued identification, criminal background check, registry forms, photograph, and fingerprinting.[6] The registrant must provide the physical address where they will be staying — not a general area, a specific address.

Some states require DNA submission from nonresident registrants. The cost of collecting the sample is at the registrant's expense.

Registration fees for nonresident visitors vary. State fees range from $10 in Alabama to $250 in Arkansas. Municipal fees can go higher — Louisiana municipalities have charged as much as $1,200.[7] These are not waivable because the person is only visiting. Budget for them.

The process itself takes longer than standard registration. Nonresident registration typically runs an hour to two hours longer than a resident update, based on the additional verification steps involved.

Residency restrictions apply to visiting registrants in 29 states. This means the address where the person plans to stay must comply with local distance requirements from schools, parks, playgrounds, or other designated locations — even for a short visit.[8] In South Carolina, the rules go further: a visiting registered person must comply with the residency restrictions of their home state while in South Carolina. A person from Ohio who cannot reside within 1,000 feet of a school or daycare cannot stay at an address in South Carolina that violates that rule, even temporarily.

Six states — Hawaii, New Mexico, North Dakota, Texas, Utah, and Washington — apply residency restrictions only to those on probation or parole, and extend those same rules to nonresident visitors who are on paper. If the visit is off paper, those restrictions do not apply in those states.

Before booking any accommodation, check the specific address against the residency restriction rules of the destination state and, in South Carolina, the home state rules as well.

## Where Registered Persons Can Sleep

Standard hotels and motels do not bar registered persons as a matter of industry policy. Unless a specific state or local law prohibits RSO accommodation at hotels — which is rare — a hotel room is available. The record does not surface at check-in. Book standard hotels and motels without concern on this point.

Short-term vacation rental platforms are a hard no. Airbnb bans registered persons outright — not with the tiered severity system applied to felons generally, but as a categorical exclusion based on registry status.[9] VRBO applies similar restrictions. This is not a policy with exceptions for time elapsed or offense type when it comes to registry status. If a name surfaces in a sex offender registry check, the booking is refused or the account is removed. Use standard hotels.

Staying at a friend's or family member's home is legally available but requires the same residency restriction analysis as any other accommodation. The relative's address must not violate local distance requirements. A cousin's house three blocks from an elementary school in a state with a 1,000-foot restriction cannot serve as a travel address, regardless of the relationship or the length of the stay.

Camping requires additional planning. Some states require a registered person to register the vehicle and campsite location when camping at public parks, and may require an itinerary.[10] Contact the home state registry office before any camping trip to understand what reporting is required, then verify with the destination jurisdiction.

## Federal Lands, National Parks, and Tribal Lands

State park bans exist in more than 18 states and apply to RSOs under varying conditions — some only to those on paper, some only to those with offenses against minors, some to all registered persons regardless of supervision status or offense type.[11]

The complication is that most federal recreation lands are governed by agencies that defer to state and local law. The Bureau of Land Management, the Army Corps of Engineers, the U.S. Forest Service, the Bureau of Reclamation, and U.S. Fish and Wildlife all permit state and local laws to apply on their lands.[12] A state park ban that covers national forests, grasslands, monuments, recreation areas, or refuges within that state can follow a registered person onto those federal lands.

The exception is the National Park Service. The NPS is the only federal land management agency that forbids state and local laws from applying on its managed lands under the Property Clause of the U.S. Constitution.[13] A state park ban does not extend to NPS-managed national parks. A registered person banned from state parks in a given state can still visit a national park located within that same state.

This distinction matters practically. Acadia, Yellowstone, the Grand Canyon, Zion, Glacier — these are NPS lands and are not subject to state park bans. National forests and recreation areas managed by the Forest Service or BLM are not NPS lands and may be subject to state law.

Tribal lands present a categorically different situation. Indigenous tribes are quasi-sovereign nations with inherent authority to exclude visitors from their lands. That authority extends to registered persons.[14] Of the 212 federally recognized tribes notified under SORNA, 198 chose to maintain their own registry functions. Each tribe with a registry operates under its own rules, which may be more restrictive than the surrounding state.

A registered person entering tribal land without understanding that tribe's rules creates serious legal exposure. The tribal registry office is the authoritative source. Contact them before visiting any tribal lands, casinos on tribal land, or reservation-adjacent areas where jurisdictional lines may be unclear.

## Private Sector: What Businesses Can and Do Refuse

Registered persons are not a protected class under federal or state civil rights law. Private businesses can refuse service for any reason, and in practice many do. There is no legal remedy when a private company declines to serve someone based on registry status.

Cruise lines are the most significant private-sector travel barrier for RSOs. Carnival and Royal Caribbean are the two lines with the longest-established and most consistently confirmed screening policies that deny boarding to registered persons.[15] Norwegian Cruise Lines implemented a ban effective June 1, 2022, confirmed by a first-person account with NCL's Director of Security Operations — the ban applies to all registry status regardless of offense type. Disney Cruise Line is also known for stringent screening and denying registered persons. Virgin Voyages was cited in earlier editions of *Your Life on the List* as banning RSOs, but this could not be independently confirmed through current sources; verify directly before booking. Holland America and MSC have been reported by travelers to permit registered persons to board, but policies can change without notice.

The prohibition does not end at the gangway for any cruise — even if a registered person boards, any port stop in a country that bars RSO entry will prevent disembarkation. The cruise becomes a floating confinement for those legs of the voyage. Verify the current policy of any cruise line directly by phone before paying for a booking.

Theme parks apply similar restrictions at select locations. Disney World in Florida bans registered persons — this is a confirmed, active policy enforced through trespass filings. Disneyland in California has not been publicly connected to such policies as of this writing. Six Flags has stated policies reserving the right to deny admission to convicted sex offenders, primarily communicated through terms on season passes; enforcement is inconsistent and the written policy predates current public corporate documentation. Verify directly with any specific Six Flags location before purchasing nonrefundable tickets.[16]

Insurance deserves mention. A 2023 survey of registered persons and their family members conducted by OnceFallen found that USAA — the insurer primarily serving veterans — was the most frequently cited company to deny service or raise rates based on registry status.[17] Other regional and national insurers were cited as well. If insurance is needed for travel, verify coverage before the trip.

The practical rule across all private-sector barriers: research before booking anything nonrefundable. Call. Check policies. Assume nothing.

## The Pre-Trip Process

This is the sequence. Run it before every out-of-state trip.

Notify the home state registry office. Provide dates, destinations, and specific addresses where possible. Document the notification — date, name of the person you spoke with, what was communicated. Do this even if the state statute does not explicitly require it.

Determine the destination state's registration threshold and how days are counted. Call the destination state registry office directly. Ask: consecutive or aggregate? Partial days? What is the exact threshold? Write down the answers and who provided them.

Verify the specific accommodation address against the destination state's residency restrictions. If the trip includes South Carolina, also check the home state restrictions. If the stay involves a friend or family member's home, apply the same analysis.

If registration will be required in the destination state, budget the time and cost. Plan to arrive in advance of any registration deadline. Bring required documentation: valid ID, home state registration documentation, and the address of the accommodation.

For camping or outdoor recreation, check whether state park bans apply and whether the land in question is NPS-managed or under a different federal agency. Contact the home state registry office about any vehicle or campsite registration requirements.

For any planned activities involving private venues — cruises, theme parks, resort bookings — verify RSO policy before paying for anything nonrefundable.

The process is methodical, not complicated. The consequences of skipping it are a new felony charge, a new conviction, and a reset on everything built since the last one.

---

## Takeaway

Domestic travel as a registered person requires compliance work that a felon off paper never has to do. The legal landscape is not uniform, not intuitive, and not forgiving of good-faith mistakes. What it is, is navigable — with preparation.

Know the home state rules before leaving. Know the destination state rules before arriving. Verify every address. Document every notification. Research every private venue.

Do the work before the trip. The alternative is doing time after it.
