# NO CLEAN SLATE
## Chapter 2: Your Supervision Status Changes Everything

---

Chapter 1 established two systems — the felony record and RSO registration. This chapter adds the third one, and it is the only one with a person behind it who can pick up the phone and send you back to prison on 24 hours' notice.

Active supervision — parole, probation, federal supervised release — is not a variation of the other two systems. It is a different kind of constraint entirely. The felony record creates barriers. The registration obligation creates ongoing requirements. Supervision creates a direct authority relationship. Someone has been assigned to monitor your compliance with a specific set of rules, and that person has real-time power over your movements, your residence, and your daily life that no statute in this book can override.

Everything that follows in this book — domestic travel, moving to a new state, international trips, expat life — assumes the reader is off paper. That assumption is stated explicitly so that anyone still under supervision knows to stop and read this chapter carefully before acting on anything else. If you are on paper, your supervision conditions govern first. The general rules come second.

This chapter explains the supervision landscape, what it does to your mobility, how to navigate moving while under it, and what the path off it looks like.

---

## What "On Paper" Means in Practice

There are three primary forms of supervision in the United States, and they operate through different legal mechanisms even though they feel similar on the ground.

**State parole** is granted by a parole board after a portion of a prison sentence has been served. The board sets conditions; a parole officer enforces them. Parole is a privilege extended by the state, not a right — and it can be revoked without the full procedural protections of a criminal trial.

**State probation** is typically ordered by a judge instead of, or in addition to, incarceration. A probation officer supervises compliance. The conditions are set at sentencing and can be modified by the court.

**Federal supervised release** replaced federal parole in 1987. After serving a federal prison sentence, most defendants serve a period of supervised release in the community. The length is set at sentencing. The conditions are set by the court and governed by federal sentencing guidelines under USSG §5D1.3. A federal probation officer handles day-to-day supervision.

The single most important thing to understand about all three: supervision conditions are *individualized*. Two people convicted of identical offenses in the same jurisdiction can walk out with completely different conditions depending on their judge, their criminal history, their probation officer's assessment, and the district's general practices. There is no single condition sheet that applies to everyone. Your conditions are your conditions. Read them. Know them. They are the only rules that matter while they are in effect.

---

## The Conditions: What They Can Restrict

Supervision conditions come in two categories: standard and discretionary.

Standard conditions apply to virtually everyone under federal supervised release. They require reporting to the probation office as directed, maintaining or seeking employment, getting permission before leaving the judicial district, avoiding excessive alcohol and any illegal drug use, and reporting law enforcement contact to the probation officer within 72 hours, among others.[1] These are the floor. Everyone on federal supervision is standing on them.

Discretionary conditions are added on top. Courts can impose any condition reasonably related to the nature of the offense, the defendant's history, or the need to protect the public — as long as the condition involves no greater restriction of liberty than necessary.[2] In practice, this gives courts and probation officers substantial latitude. Discretionary conditions can include curfews, home detention, community service, internet and social media bans, polygraph requirements, occupational restrictions, and prohibitions on contact with specific individuals or categories of people.

For people convicted of sex offenses, the guidelines add a specific mandatory layer on top of the standard conditions.[3] These include participation in a sex offense treatment and monitoring program at the defendant's own expense, potential computer and internet restrictions if the offense involved those tools, and a warrantless search condition allowing law enforcement to search the person, property, vehicle, electronic devices, and residence upon reasonable suspicion of a violation.

Those costs are not hypothetical. One person on Tennessee's community supervision for life documented paying $95 per month for supervision and GPS monitoring, $850 for a one-time psychosexual evaluation, $175 twice a year for polygraphs, $40 per week for sex offense treatment, and $150 annually in registry fees — a total of roughly $4,200 in the first year alone. Failure to pay supervision fees is itself a violation. People have gone back to prison for not being able to afford the cost of being supervised.

The PO also has discretionary power independent of the court. Standard conditions in many states explicitly authorize the supervising officer to add rules as they see fit. That final clause — the PO can modify conditions at any time — is not bureaucratic language. It is real authority. A PO who decides you should not be allowed to attend a specific event, stay overnight somewhere new, or use a particular platform has the power to make that prohibition effective immediately. Whether that exercise of authority is legally sound is a separate question from whether it will be enforced while you're arguing about it.

## How Supervision Restricts Travel

The baseline travel restriction under federal supervised release is that the defendant shall not leave the judicial district without permission from the court or probation officer.[4] That is not the state — it is the federal judicial district, which is often considerably smaller. The Southern District of New York, for example, covers Manhattan and a handful of surrounding counties. Someone under federal supervision there cannot drive to New Jersey without approval.

State parole and probation operate similarly, though the geographic boundaries vary by state. In most cases, travel outside the county or a defined region requires advance notice and explicit permission. The definition of "travel" is not always limited to overnight trips. A day trip to visit family in another county may require approval.

What approval looks like in practice depends on the PO. Some will give verbal approval and leave it at that. That is a problem if the approval is ever disputed. Get travel permission in writing — an email confirmation, a signed travel permit, a text message you screenshot and date. If something goes wrong while you are traveling, a verbal agreement with a PO who no longer remembers the conversation is worth nothing. A paper trail is the only protection you have.

Overnight stays require more documentation than day trips. Traveling across state lines requires more still. For RSOs, any travel that could trigger registration requirements in the destination state adds another layer of compliance. Chapter 4 covers RSO domestic travel registration rules in detail. The point here is simpler: while on paper, the travel permission process has to start with the PO before any other consideration applies.

International travel while on supervision is, for most people, functionally impossible. The reasons stack: standard conditions prohibit leaving the judicial district without permission, POs are unlikely to approve foreign travel, the 21-day advance notice requirement for RSOs involves federal coordination that supervision conditions complicate, and most passports cannot be obtained or are conditioned during supervision. It is not a legal impossibility in every case — it is a practical impossibility in most. If international travel is a priority, the realistic path runs through getting off paper first.

## Moving While on Paper: State Supervision and the Interstate Compact

Moving to a new state while on state parole or probation requires an Interstate Compact. There is no workaround for this. You cannot simply give notice, pack up, and report to a new probation office in another state. Both states have to agree to the transfer through the Interstate Commission for Adult Offender Supervision (ICAOS), and the transfer has to be approved before you move.[5]

The process works like this: the sending state submits a transfer request to the receiving state. The receiving state has a set window to review the proposed residence and respond. If the receiving state approves, it issues reporting instructions. If it denies, you stay put. For RSOs, ICAOS rules prohibit travel permits pending a transfer request — which means an RSO cannot even make a scouting trip to the receiving state while the transfer application is being processed.[6]

What the receiving state is evaluating: whether the proposed residence is one that a similarly situated person sentenced in that state would be permitted to live at. If the answer is no — because of residency restrictions, proximity to schools, or local ordinance — the transfer will be denied regardless of how clean your record is. Research the specific rules of the destination state before filing a transfer request. Chapter 6 covers RSO state-by-state considerations in depth.

The criteria for transfer approval follow a general pattern, illustrated by Florida's published standards: more than 90 days of supervision remaining, a valid supervision plan, substantial compliance with current conditions, and either state residency for at least a year before the offense or qualifying family ties in the receiving state — meaning a parent, grandparent, adult sibling, spouse, or legal guardian who has lived in the receiving state for at least 180 days and is willing and able to assist.[7]

The approval rate is meaningful: over two-thirds of interstate transfer requests are granted. The most common reason for rejection is not the applicant's record — it is inadequate paperwork. More than 72% of case rejections in Fiscal Year 2022 resulted from failure to verify transfer plans or provide sufficient justification for the request.[8] Do the documentation work before submitting. Verify the residence, confirm family contacts will follow through, and make sure the receiving state's rules have been checked against the proposed address.

Once approved, the compact operates as a simultaneous agreement with both states. You are subject to the rules of the sending state *and* the receiving state at the same time. Either state can revoke the compact. If revoked, you return to the sending state to finish the supervision period. Understand this before committing to a life in a new location — the receiving state's rules may be more restrictive than where you started.

## Moving While on Paper: Federal Supervised Release

Federal supervised release does not run through ICAOS. Federal transfers are handled internally through the probation office system, and the geography that matters is the federal judicial district, not the state.

Moving within the same federal district is the simpler scenario. You still need PO approval. The PO will investigate the new address — confirm it exists, run background checks on everyone living there, verify that no one at the address has a felony conviction (a standard federal condition prohibits residing with anyone who has been convicted of a felony), confirm that housemates understand and accept the search condition attached to your supervision. If the address clears, the move can be approved relatively quickly.

Moving to a different federal district is a longer process. The first step worth attempting is asking for courtesy supervision — an arrangement where your case stays in the original district but a probation officer in the new district agrees to supervise you locally. Both POs have to agree. It is not guaranteed, but when it works it is faster and lower-friction than a formal transfer. If courtesy supervision is arranged first, a formal transfer later becomes significantly easier.

A formal cross-district transfer requires the sending district to submit a Transfer Investigation to the receiving district. The receiving district then investigates the proposed address, runs the same checks as any within-district move, and either approves or denies within approximately 30 days. The receiving district can deny for any reason. If denied, supervision stays in the original district. The sending district cannot act until the receiving district responds.[9]

One practical note on timing: the Transfer Investigation takes at minimum 30 days, often longer. If a housing opportunity has a deadline, that deadline is almost certainly incompatible with the federal transfer process. Plan further ahead than feels necessary.

## When Supervision Is Violated

Violations come in two categories: technical violations and new criminal conduct. Both can send you back.

A technical violation is a failure to comply with a supervision condition that is not itself a crime. Missing a PO check-in. Failing to report a law enforcement contact. Traveling without permission. Testing positive for a prohibited substance. Failing to attend mandatory treatment. Failing to pay supervision fees. These do not require a conviction — the government needs to prove a violation by a preponderance of the evidence, a standard considerably lower than beyond a reasonable doubt.[10]

The return-to-prison rate for people on supervision is not driven primarily by new offenses. A 2014 study of California sex offense registrants released from prison found that among those who returned within three years, 91.9% returned on a parole violation, 5.3% returned on a new non-sex crime, 2% returned for Failure to Register, and only 0.8% returned for a new sex offense.[11] The rules — not new crimes — are what put most people back inside.

False allegations of violations carry the same immediate consequences as real ones. An accusation triggers detention. There may be no pre-hearing release. Months can pass before a hearing, during which housing, employment, and other stability built since release can collapse. The burden of defending against an allegation of a violation falls on the defendant in practical terms even if the legal standard theoretically falls on the government.

The lesson is not paranoia — it is precision. Know your conditions. Document compliance. Get approvals in writing. When in doubt, ask before acting. The cost of asking permission is a phone call. The cost of moving without permission can be a revocation hearing.

## Challenging and Terminating Supervision

Conditions can be challenged, but the process is slow and the bar is high. The first option — complaining to the PO's supervisor or filing with state police or the FBI at the federal level — rarely produces results. The realistic path is through the court.

To challenge a condition, the argument is that the condition is not reasonably related to relevant sentencing factors, is more restrictive than necessary to serve its stated purpose, or is unconstitutional on its face. Appellate courts have struck down conditions on each of these grounds — conditions that were vague and overbroad, conditions that restricted liberty beyond what the offense or defendant's history could justify, and conditions that violated constitutional protections.[12] The possibility of a successful challenge is real. It is not common, and it is not fast. An attorney familiar with supervised release conditions in your district is the starting point.

Early termination of federal supervised release is available under 18 U.S.C. § 3583(e)(1) after one year of supervision has been completed. The court can terminate if it is satisfied that early termination is warranted by the defendant's conduct and the interests of justice. The sentencing guidelines explicitly encourage courts to use this authority in appropriate cases.[13] The Sixth Circuit has held that courts may terminate supervised release early even when the statute of conviction specified a particular term — the judge has discretion.

What builds a case for early termination: clean drug tests throughout, consistent and timely reporting, completed treatment requirements, stable employment, stable housing, no new law enforcement contact, and no technical violations. The pattern matters more than any single factor. A motion for early termination supported by a strong compliance record over time is different from the same motion filed without it.

State early termination rules vary considerably. Some states have defined processes and criteria; others leave it to judicial discretion with minimal guidance. If early termination is a goal, learn the specific rules for the state and court that imposed supervision.

## Coming Off Paper: What Changes, What Doesn't

When supervision ends — whether by completing the full term, earning early termination, or having conditions expire — the PO's authority ends with it. Immediately. No more check-ins. No more travel permission. No more approval required before moving. That layer of the system simply lifts.

What does not change is everything underneath it. The felony record remains. For RSOs, every registration obligation remains in full force. Coming off paper does not reset a registration period, change a tier classification, or reduce check-in frequency. The supervision system and the registration system are independent. Ending one has no effect on the other.

This surprises people. It should not, but it does — often enough that it's worth stating plainly. Getting off paper is a significant milestone. It is not the end of the compliance picture for anyone on the registry.

The first 30 days after supervision ends are worth treating carefully. Update your address with any relevant agencies that tracked your supervision. Confirm with your former PO's office in writing that supervision has been terminated and request documentation of that termination. If you are an RSO, confirm your registration status and reporting schedule with the registration office — supervision ending does not alter those obligations, but the transition period creates administrative confusion that can lead to missed deadlines. Keep a dated record of when supervision ended and what you did to confirm it. That record matters if any future agency, employer, or foreign government asks about the status of your supervision.

After that, the rest of this book applies. The felony record and registration obligations are navigable. They require planning, information, and attention. They do not require a PO's approval.

---

## Takeaway

Supervision is the most immediately powerful of the three systems that affect people with criminal records. While it is active, it governs everything else. Travel, relocation, international options — all of it runs through supervision conditions first.

The good news is that supervision ends. The record and, for RSOs, the registration obligation do not end automatically, but supervision does — either by completing the term or by earning early termination. The path to early termination runs through consistent compliance, completed treatment, and a clean record. It is not guaranteed, but it is available and worth pursuing.

Once off paper, the constraints in this book are the constraints that remain. They are manageable. The chapters that follow explain how.
